# Mark Cuban

**Era:** 1983–present · **Legacy:** Self-made tech entrepreneur, sports owner, and prolific operator-investor · **Section:** Business Icons

## Overview

Mark Cuban started selling garbage bags door-to-door at twelve, ran bars and disco parties in college, built MicroSolutions in his 20s (sold to CompuServe for $6M in 1990), then co-founded Broadcast.com which sold to Yahoo! for $5.7B in 1999. He bought the Dallas Mavericks in 2000, won an NBA championship in 2011, and joined ABC's *Shark Tank* in 2011 where he became the most aggressive and most empirically successful Shark across the show's run. Active operator into the 2020s with Cost Plus Drugs and other ventures.

## Core philosophy

**"Sales cures all."** Cuban's most repeated line. Marketing, product, hiring, fundraising, strategy — every operational problem softens when revenue is climbing. He's also relentless on **customer obsession** ("Treat your customers like they own you, because they do") and on the value of **doing the boring work** competitors won't do.

## Signature lessons for sellers

- **Cost discipline.** Cuban famously ran MicroSolutions and early Broadcast.com on extreme expense austerity. Profit margin is built from inputs, not just price.
- **Outwork competitors on basics.** "One of the most underrated skills in business right now is being nice. Nice sells." Boring, repeatable, courteous.
- **Information advantage.** Read everything in your industry. Cuban claims his reading habit (3+ hours daily) is his actual edge.
- **Owner mentality for sellers.** Think about what *you* would do if you were the prospect's CFO — then sell from that vantage.
- **Time arbitrage.** Most people aren't willing to grind for years on the same boring thing — which is exactly why doing so produces outliers.

## Puerto Rico application

Cuban's cost-discipline + customer-obsession + boring-execution combination is **a near-perfect mental model for PR small business**, where margins are tight and customer concentration is high. Two specific lessons translate hard: (1) the **"nice sells" doctrine** — PR business culture rewards courtesy and relationship investment; Cuban's framing makes "being nice" a competitive strategy, not a soft skill; (2) the **information-edge habit** — most PR market segments are under-researched relative to their mainland equivalents; a seller who reads everything published about a specific PR vertical (insurance, hospitality, healthcare, construction) can build genuine expertise advantage in months.
