# 06 — Puerto Rico Application

> The local-market adaptation layer. This file is where the universal psychology and modern frameworks meet the bilingual, relationship-driven, high-trust, high-warmth reality of selling in Puerto Rico.

Everything in the other five strategy files holds globally. This file is where you tune the dials for the island.

---

## 1. The Cultural Foundation

### Confianza as the operating system

*Confianza* is not "trust" — it's the social infrastructure underneath any meaningful commerce. Selling without it is technically possible and operationally fragile.

Confianza is built through:
- **Time invested without an ask attached.**
- **Showing up in person** — for non-sales events, not just deal moments.
- **Vouching from a known third party** (padrino, conocido en común).
- **Demonstrated discretion.** Local business communities are dense; talk travels fast.
- **Consistent presence over years**, not weeks.

### The pana / compadre layer

Mid-market and SMB deals frequently close at the *pana* layer — an inner ring of trusted relationships. Outsiders gain access through introduction, never through cold outreach alone. This is why dream-100 (Holmes) and the padrino route consistently outperform pure outbound in this market.

### Family-business reality

A large share of PR private-sector businesses are family-owned or family-controlled. The visible decision-maker is rarely the only decision-maker. Spouses, parents, siblings, and adult children frequently influence or veto deals.

Operational implication:
- Map the *informal* org chart in your CRM, not just the title chart.
- Don't push for a same-day signature on family-business deals — it triggers internal resistance.
- The follow-up after a family consultation is often the actual closing meeting.

## 2. The Bilingual Dynamic

### Language matching as a respect signal

The seller who matches the buyer's natural language flow signals fluency in their world. Mismatches read as friction.

**Heuristics that hold up in practice:**

| Buyer profile | Dominant language for sales conversation |
|---|---|
| Retail / hospitality / food / construction | Spanish, with English technical terms mixed |
| Tech, finance, Act-60 relocators | English, with light Spanish for warmth |
| Government, education, healthcare (non-clinical) | Spanish primary, formal register |
| Federal contracting, biotech, manufacturing | English primary, with Spanish for floor-level relationships |
| Family-owned mid-market | Spanish for the family, English for the next-gen operators — switch fluidly |

### Spanglish in commercial context

Natural code-switching is *expected* and read as fluency. Forcing English-only in a Spanish-comfortable room is cold. Forcing Spanish where the buyer prefers English is patronizing. Read the room and mirror.

### Written collateral

- **Contracts:** English is legally standard in formal commerce. Bilingual versions are courtesy, not requirement.
- **Marketing materials:** Spanish-primary for SMB, English-primary for relocator and federal verticals.
- **Translations:** never machine-only. A poorly translated Spanish landing page reads worse than English alone.

## 3. Timing & Cadence

### Calendar awareness

- **December 15 – January 6 (Navidades extended):** business slows dramatically. Closes that don't land before mid-December usually push to mid-January. Plan accordingly.
- **Semana Santa (Holy Week):** typically dead for new initiatives.
- **Mother's Day weekend:** family-business owners are out. Don't push closes.
- **Summer (June–August):** mixed — some industries surge (insurance, hurricane prep, school-year setup), others slow (financial services, real estate transactions for relocators).
- **September–October:** post-hurricane-season planning surge for B2B.
- **November:** Q4 budget-spend window, especially in tech and services.

### Hurricane season operational reality

June 1 – November 30. Local buyers pre-position for storms. This drives demand surges in:
- Backup power and generators
- Telecommunications redundancy
- Insurance review and supplement
- Inventory and supply chain risk solutions
- Cloud / off-island data backup
- Construction and repair services

### Daily timing

- **9:00–10:30** — best for cold calls (people are at their desks, pre-meeting load).
- **10:30–12:00** — best for warm follow-ups.
- **12:00–2:00** — lunch is sacred. Schedule sales lunches; don't cold-call.
- **2:00–3:30** — slow period, lighter response.
- **3:30–5:30** — second best window for follow-ups.
- **Friday after 2:00** — minimal traction; reserve for weekly internal review.

## 4. Channels Adapted

### WhatsApp Business

The single most underutilized channel by outside sellers, the single most-used channel by local SMB buyers. Standard practice in PR commerce:

- Voice notes are normal and warm. Don't avoid them.
- Replies expected within hours, not days.
- WhatsApp Business catalog feature is functionally a mini-storefront for many local sellers.
- A WhatsApp number on your business card outperforms any other contact field for SMB conversion.

### Email

Works well for relocator, federal, mid-market formal verticals. Substantially weaker for SMB unless preceded by phone or referral.

### LinkedIn

Reasonable penetration in tech, finance, federal contracting, executive search. Low penetration in retail, food, construction, traditional services. Use selectively.

### Phone

Still the highest-trust direct channel. Local numbers (787 / 939) outperform mainland numbers materially on pickup rate.

### In person

Not optional for mid-market and above. Quarterly check-ins, chamber events, conferences, sector breakfasts. The PR seller's calendar is heavier on in-person time than the mainland equivalent.

## 5. Vertical-Specific Notes

### Auto

High trust-cycle market. Repeat customers and family referrals dominate. Negotiation is expected — don't quote your floor. Closes often involve multiple visits over a week.

### Real estate

Heavily relationship-driven. Top producers earn 60%+ of business from referrals. Bilingual capacity is decisive in the relocator segment. Negotiation rounds are standard.

### Insurance

Family-friend relationship sales. Switching is rare and high-friction; therefore acquisition is high-touch. Hurricane season is the largest annual demand window.

### Professional services (legal, accounting, consulting)

Referral-led almost exclusively. Outbound marketing has limited ROI compared to chamber participation and professional-association presence.

### Retail / e-commerce

WhatsApp-driven. Instagram and Facebook ads are the primary discovery channels. Local pickup options matter (logistics costs and reliability constraints).

### Hospitality

Volume + seasonality. Sales conversations move quickly during peak booking windows. Relationship capital with travel agents and event planners is the multiplier.

### Construction / contractors

Word-of-mouth dominant. Project references are everything. Permit-and-licensing literacy is a credibility signal — speak the local regulatory vocabulary.

### Tech / Act-60 / relocators

English-primary. Mainland-pricing expectations. Premium positioning. Discovery cycle is shorter than local-incumbent; trust is built through credentials and case studies, not through years of presence.

### Healthcare (non-clinical commerce)

Provider relationships matter. Negotiation occurs through administrators, not clinicians. Long deal cycles.

### Public sector / government

Slow. Documentation-heavy. Procurement processes are well-codified. Compliance literacy (federal funds, single audit, transparency requirements) is a baseline expectation.

## 6. Regulatory Vocabulary

Fluency in these terms signals seriousness:

- **CRIM** — Centro de Recaudación de Ingresos Municipales (municipal tax)
- **IVU** — Impuesto sobre Ventas y Uso (sales tax, 11.5% in most of PR)
- **Hacienda** — Department of Treasury
- **DDEC** — Departamento de Desarrollo Económico y Comercio
- **Acta 60 / Act 60** — consolidated tax-incentive framework
- **Act 20 / Act 22** — predecessor incentives for export services and individual investors
- **OCIF** — Oficina del Comisionado de Instituciones Financieras
- **PROMESA / Junta** — federal oversight board
- **Permisos** — DDEC and municipal permitting
- **JCA** — Junta de Calidad Ambiental (environmental quality)
- **Patente municipal** — municipal business license
- **ASES / Plan de Salud** — government healthcare plan

Misuse or mispronunciation of these signals an outsider immediately. Use correctly, or use English equivalents.

## 7. Pricing & Negotiation Adapted

- **Expect a counter.** Local mid-market and SMB buyers negotiate as standard. Build a ~10% margin into your published number.
- **Round numbers are fine in Spanish-language quotes.** Voss's rule on precise numbers still helps in English; in Spanish-primary commerce, the round number reads as cleaner.
- **Net terms expectations:** net-30 to net-60 are common in B2B. Net-15 reads as aggressive locally.
- **Cash and ACH dominate** below mid-market. Credit-card-only acceptance limits your addressable market in many verticals.
- **Discount as gesture, not as principle.** A small discount at the close, framed as a personal accommodation, lands warmer than a calculated tier discount.

## 8. Common Failure Modes (External Sellers)

1. **Treating PR like a US-mainland market.** It isn't. Trust cycles, channels, holidays, language norms differ enough to invalidate playbooks that work elsewhere.
2. **Over-indexing on LinkedIn.** Underpenetrated in most local verticals. Misses 70%+ of decision-makers.
3. **Email-only sequences.** Below 10% reply rate in SMB. WhatsApp + phone substantially outperform.
4. **Aggressive urgency closes.** Read as *falta de respeto*. Burn long-term capital for short-term close.
5. **Spanish translations done by non-native or non-PR translators.** Spain-Spanish or generic Latin-American Spanish reads slightly off. PR-Spanish is its own register.
6. **Ignoring the family layer in family-owned businesses.**
7. **Quoting your floor price first.** Removes the negotiation room buyers expect.
8. **Missing the cultural calendar** — booking key events during Semana Santa, Navidades, or Mother's Day weekend.
9. **Speaking only with the visible decision-maker** when the actual decision is made elsewhere in the family or padrino circle.
10. **Failing to follow through on small commitments.** Local commerce remembers exactly who delivered the document, the introduction, the recipe they said they would send.

## 9. The PR-Adapted Sales Cadence (Composite)

```
Week 1
  - Initial outreach via warm intro if possible, phone + WhatsApp if not
  - First meeting: in-person if at all viable

Week 2
  - Discovery call / Spanish or English per buyer comfort
  - SPIN + Challenger mix
  - Identify family / padrino / coach layer

Week 3
  - Proposal — bilingual or buyer-language version
  - In-person delivery if mid-market+, courier-quality presentation

Week 4
  - Family / advisor consultation window — patience here is the trust signal
  - Voss-style follow-up with calibrated questions, not pressure

Week 5
  - Close meeting, often with second stakeholder present
  - Document immediately, send recap same day

Week 6+
  - Onboarding with high-touch presence
  - 30/60/90 check-ins
  - Referral ask at peak satisfaction
```

## 10. The Long-Term PR Sales Career

Patterns observed across high performers in the PR market over 10+ year careers:

- **Concentrated industry depth beats wide breadth.** Top closers usually pick 1–2 verticals and dominate them.
- **Chamber and association leadership** is a multiplier — board service in a relevant body materially affects deal flow within 3 years.
- **Bilingual fluency is non-negotiable** at the top. Even English-primary sellers need conversational Spanish for relationship maintenance.
- **The book-of-business compounds slowly, then suddenly.** Years 1–4 feel disproportionately hard. Years 5–10 feel disproportionately easy if value was deposited correctly.
- **Reputation is your only true asset.** It travels faster here than in any other market this archive's sources describe. Protect it accordingly.
