# 06 — Puerto Rico Application

*The local-market adaptation layer. Every framework in the previous five playbooks gets calibrated here for the realities of selling in Puerto Rico.*

## Why this playbook exists

Most sales literature is written from a US mainland perspective. It assumes English-default communication, low-context business culture, fast trust cycles, transactional decision-making, and a wide-open consumer market. Puerto Rico is none of those things — it's bilingual, high-context, relationship-anchored, family-business-dense, and operating in a 3.2M-person market where reputation propagates faster than any CRM. Sellers who import mainland playbooks verbatim consistently underperform. Sellers who calibrate get outlier results. This playbook is the calibration layer.

## The cultural operating system

Five concepts that aren't translatable, but must be internalized:

### *Confianza*
Not "trust" — a deeper, slower, earned-through-repetition concept. *Confianza* is the precondition for every transaction above a certain threshold, and it doesn't transfer through credentials, websites, or testimonials at the same rate it does through personal vouching. Build it slowly. Burn it once and you don't get it back.

### *Resolver*
Literally "to resolve" — in PR business culture it means making problems go away regardless of process. The seller who can *resolver* outperforms the seller with the best feature list. Engineer your offer so the buyer experiences *resolver* in the first 30 days.

### *Cariño*
Warmth, affection, genuine care. Not optional in PR business interactions — the absence of *cariño* is read as either coldness or disrespect. *"Don José, ¿cómo está la familia?"* is not small talk. It's a status check on the relationship.

### *Ser profesional*
Professional, in the local sense — not "corporate." Punctuality (within local norms), follow-through on commitments, dressing appropriately for the context, knowing your material cold. The bar is real. Sloppy professionalism gets you eliminated; performative corporate stiffness gets you tolerated, not chosen.

### *Palabra*
Your word. In PR business, a verbal yes from the *dueño* is often functionally binding before paper is signed — and a seller who breaks their *palabra* loses not just one deal but their reputation across the network within weeks.

## Language: bilingual operating rules

### When to lead in Spanish
- *Boricua*-owned businesses (any size)
- Family businesses regardless of generation
- Older decision-makers (50+) even at mainland-headquartered firms
- Service and hospitality industries
- Government and institutional buyers
- Most B2C across the island

### When to lead in English
- Mainland-headquartered subsidiaries (pharma, manufacturing, US-chartered banks)
- Younger tech and finance professionals who explicitly prefer it
- Act 60 inbound clients from the diaspora
- International venture-backed companies operating from PR

### Code-switching rules
- **Mirror the buyer.** They open in Spanish, you stay in Spanish. They flip to English mid-conversation, you flip with them.
- **Don't translate jargon literally.** *"Optimizar el funnel de conversión"* sounds non-native. *"Mejorar cómo se cierran más ventas"* sounds like a *boricua* professional.
- **Numbers in the buyer's preferred language.** Prices, dates, and figures spoken in the language the buyer is thinking in reduce friction.
- **Bilingual proposals matter.** For institutional deals, send the executive summary in Spanish and the detailed appendix in English (or vice versa, mirroring the buyer's preference).

## The trust cycle

Mainland B2B SMB sales averages roughly 2–6 weeks from first touch to closed deal for $5–20K ACV. PR averages 4–12 weeks for the same ticket. Add 30–60% to the cycle on top of mainland norms and plan accordingly.

**Why it's longer:**
- More stakeholders consulted informally (spouse, accountant, primary referral source)
- Higher *confianza* threshold required before commitment
- Cultural preference for in-person meetings on consequential decisions
- Family-business dynamics: decisions often pause around family events, holidays, and patron-saint festivals

**What to do about it:**
- Don't compress artificially. Pressure tactics shorten nothing and burn relationships.
- Use the extra time to deepen value-delivery (audits, intros, free insight) so reciprocity accumulates.
- Stay top-of-mind via WhatsApp light-touches (holiday wishes, *Día de Reyes, Acción de Gracias, Navidad*, hurricane check-ins).
- Be the seller who shows up after *María*, after *Fiona*, after every event. Reputation is built in the inconvenient moments.

## Industry verticals: the practical map

### Insurance and financial services
- Buyer behavior: chronic under-insurance; trust-anchored; price-sensitive on monthlies but value-sensitive on coverage clarity.
- Strategy: implication questions (SPIN) about post-hurricane recovery; value-based reframing of coverage as *resolver* for the family.
- Cultural notes: *don/doña* register matters; family-meeting closings work; *consultar con la esposa/esposo* is real, not a stall.
- Top channels: phone + in-person + referral. WhatsApp for ongoing service.

### Real estate and construction
- Buyer behavior: long cycles; family-stakeholder-heavy; post-disaster construction wave is structural, not cyclical.
- Strategy: Dream 100 on developers and project owners; multi-touch relationship investment.
- Cultural notes: *dueño* often signs personally even on corporate deals; respect the *cuento* (the story) of the property.
- Top channels: in-person + phone + chamber events.

### Automotive
- Buyer behavior: family-driven decision; multiple-visit norm; financing-driven affordability framing.
- Strategy: Hopkins-style reduction-to-the-ridiculous (monthly payment framing); Girard-style follow-up discipline for repeat and referral business.
- Cultural notes: family test-drives are common; selling to the *abuela* who's funding the down payment is real.
- Top channels: walk-in + referral + Meta ads + WhatsApp.

### Hospitality and tourism
- Buyer behavior: highly seasonal; relationship-dense local supplier networks; mainland chains vs local *empresarios* play different games.
- Strategy: Branson-style service-experience differentiation; Go-Giver positioning with supplier and operator networks.
- Cultural notes: *amabilidad* is baseline; productizing service consistency is the differentiator.
- Top channels: in-person + industry events + LinkedIn for B2B vendor sales.

### Professional services (legal, accounting, advisory)
- Buyer behavior: judgment-based selection; referral-dominant pipeline; long-term relationship retention.
- Strategy: Challenger model (Dixon & Adamson); value-based selling at the high end; thought leadership content in Spanish.
- Cultural notes: degrees and certifications signal competence; track record of similar local clients signals *confianza*.
- Top channels: referral + LinkedIn + bar/chamber networks + content.

### B2B SaaS and tech
- Buyer behavior: local SMB skeptical of mainland-only vendors; institutional buyers comfortable with both.
- Strategy: lead with bilingual support, local CSM, local case studies; hurricane-resilient deployment as a real differentiator.
- Cultural notes: many mainland SaaS vendors lose PR deals on perceived support gaps — win them with named local support contracts.
- Top channels: outbound email + LinkedIn + warm intros via local tech network.

### Healthcare and wellness
- Buyer behavior: regulatory-heavy; insurance-driven economics; family decision-making.
- Strategy: outcome-specific selling (concrete metrics, defined timelines); compliance-clear messaging.
- Cultural notes: the *doctor de cabecera* tradition is alive; bedside manner translates to sales presence.
- Top channels: referral + in-person + targeted content.

## Regulatory and structural realities (2026)

- **Act 60** continues to drive inbound from US and international high-net-worth individuals seeking tax incentives. Sellers operating in real estate, financial services, accounting, legal, and lifestyle services (concierge, security, education) should have an Act 60-fluent client track.
- **Hurricane season** (June–November) compresses or pauses many sales cycles. Plan campaigns around it; avoid pushing closes in the active days of a major storm; show up immediately after with check-ins, not pitches.
- **Power and connectivity reliability** continues to vary across the island. Service businesses with backup power capability and offline-resilient operations have a real competitive advantage — lead with this when relevant.
- **Bilingual contracts** are standard for institutional deals. Have both versions reviewed by a PR-licensed attorney; don't rely on machine translation.
- **Sales tax (IVU)** and other regulatory specifics shift periodically — always verify current law before quoting tax-inclusive prices. Don't guess.

## The communication channel reality (2026)

| Channel | PR weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| WhatsApp | Very high | The dominant business communication channel for SMB and personal-relationship sales. Build systematic templates and sequences. |
| Phone | High | Older buyers prefer voice; call mid-morning and mid-afternoon, avoid lunch hours and after 5 PM. |
| In-person | High | Trust-heavy categories still require face time. Industry events, chambers, professional associations. |
| Email | Medium | Works for institutional and corporate buyers; lower response rates than mainland for SMB. |
| LinkedIn | Medium | Growing for B2B professional services and Act 60 inbound. |
| Meta (FB/IG) | High | Especially in Spanish; CPMs typically 30–60% below mainland equivalents. |
| TikTok | High and growing | Younger demographics; Spanish content underserved relative to demand. |
| YouTube | Medium | Considered purchases; long-form Spanish PR content is wide-open. |
| Radio | Medium | Still relevant for older demographics and morning-commute reach. |

## The cadence (PR-calibrated 7-touch)

Adjust the canonical Blount cadence as follows for PR SMB outreach:

- **Day 1:** WhatsApp message + LinkedIn connection request
- **Day 4:** Phone call (mid-morning); voicemail only on second attempt
- **Day 7:** Email with attached audit/insight
- **Day 11:** WhatsApp light follow-up
- **Day 16:** In-person drop-by if geography allows, or video email
- **Day 22:** Phone + brief WhatsApp recap
- **Day 30:** Polite "breakup" message; re-enter at top in 60–90 days

Key calibrations: stretched cadence (30 days vs mainland 15), WhatsApp at multiple stages, in-person built in at day 16.

## The seller's daily standard (PR full-time hunter)

- **6:00–7:30 AM** — personal development time (audio: Robbins, Ziglar, Tracy, ET) + morning routine
- **7:30–8:30** — plan the day, review the pipeline, identify the 3 priority moves
- **8:30–11:30** — prospecting block (untouchable): outbound calls + WhatsApp + email + LinkedIn touches
- **11:30–12:30** — admin, CRM updates, proposal work
- **12:30–2:00** — lunch meetings (often the highest-leverage time slot in PR)
- **2:00–5:00** — booked meetings, demos, in-person visits
- **5:00–6:00** — wrap-up, write tomorrow's plan, send last touches
- **6:00 PM onward** — family. Respect the local cultural boundary; sellers who don't burn out within 18 months.

## Common failure modes to avoid

1. **Importing mainland intensity verbatim.** Cardone-level aggression on a *don José* family-business call loses the deal in 90 seconds.
2. **Treating WhatsApp like personal text.** Sloppy grammar, voice notes without warning, late-night messages — all signal *poco profesional*.
3. **Skipping the relational opening.** Going straight to pitch is the #1 unforced error. 90 seconds of *¿cómo está la familia?* changes everything.
4. **Reflexive discounting.** When asked *"¿Me das un descuentito?"* the value-seller reopens value, not price.
5. **Disappearing post-close.** The deal is the beginning, not the end. Girard's monthly touch discipline applies double here.
6. **Mismatching English/Spanish register to the buyer.** Read the room. Mirror their language. Switch when they switch.
7. **Promising what *resolver* you can't deliver.** Better to say *"déjame ver y te aviso mañana"* than to over-commit. Broken *palabra* costs more than missed deals.

## Pulling it all together

The five preceding playbooks teach the universal craft: psychology, prospecting, closing, offer creation, value-based selling. This playbook teaches the local *register*. A seller who masters all six — the universal craft plus the PR calibration — operates at a level that's structurally rare on the island: globally informed, locally fluent, technically disciplined, relationally warm.

That seller doesn't get hired by competitors. They get partnered with.
