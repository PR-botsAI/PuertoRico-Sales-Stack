# 03 — Closing and Negotiation

*The conversation where value gets exchanged. Most sellers under-prepare this and let the buyer's process control the close. This playbook reverses that.*

## Why this playbook exists

Closing is not a single moment; it's the final stage of a process whose outcome was largely determined upstream. A great close on a poorly qualified lead produces a bad customer; a clumsy close on a perfect lead still produces revenue. The skill is **knowing which stage you're actually in**, and applying the right instrument.

## The four phases of the close

### Phase 1 — Trial close (mid-conversation)
A trial close tests temperature without forcing a decision. Examples: *"Based on what you've heard so far, does this seem like it could solve the X problem?"* / *"What questions are still open for you?"* Voss's calibrated questions live here. The yes you get isn't binding — it's diagnostic.

### Phase 2 — Summary close
Recap the buyer's stated needs, your proposed fit, and the value math. Use *their words*, not yours — the closer the summary mirrors how they described the problem, the higher the conversion. "Así mismo es" is the signal you're looking for.

### Phase 3 — The ask
Direct, calm, specific. The single biggest closing mistake is hedging: "So, uh, would you maybe want to think about possibly moving forward?" kills conversion. Replace with: *"Are you ready to move forward this week, or would you prefer we start next month?"* Two-option assumptive close — Hopkins's signature.

### Phase 4 — Objection handling
Objections are not rejections — they're requests for more information or reassurance. The order of operations: **acknowledge → isolate → reframe → ask again**.

## The objection framework

### The five universal objections (Ziglar)

1. **No need** — you sold to the wrong person; revisit qualification.
2. **No money** — split into "can't afford" (real) vs "won't prioritize" (positioning); handle differently.
3. **No hurry** — introduce a cost-of-inaction frame or genuine deadline.
4. **No desire** — you haven't connected to the emotional driver; revisit discovery.
5. **No trust** — the hardest; usually fixed by social proof, guarantee, or smaller commitment first.

### Tactical empathy (Voss) for objections

- **Label first:** "It sounds like the timing is the main concern." — names the emotion, drops the defense.
- **Then mirror:** repeat the last 1–3 words of their objection. They expand. The real objection often emerges.
- **Then calibrated question:** "How am I supposed to make this work for you?" — moves them into solving their own objection.
- **Avoid:** "Yes, but…" — it nullifies everything before the "but." Use "Yes, and…" or just "Help me understand…"

## Frame control during close (Klaff)

The buyer will attempt status moves: late arrival, distraction, dismissive comments, surprise approvers added at the last minute. Frame-collapse signals: you start over-explaining, accepting last-minute scope changes, dropping price reflexively. Hold frame by: **slowing your pace, refusing reactive concessions, naming the dynamic** (*"Parece que estamos revisando esto bajo otro ángulo — ¿qué cambió?"*), and being willing to walk.

## Negotiation principles (Voss + Klaff + Caan)

1. **The first number anchors.** Whoever names the first number sets the negotiation's gravity. Decide whether to anchor first (when you know the market) or pull anchor from them (when you don't).
2. **Concessions in shrinking increments.** $5K → $3K → $1K → $200 signals you're approaching your floor. Equal-sized concessions ($2K → $2K → $2K) signal there's more to give.
3. **Always trade.** Never concede without getting something back — longer term, larger scope, faster decision, payment terms, referrals, case study rights.
4. **"That's right" is the close.** Voss: when the buyer says "that's right," they've internalized your framing. "You're right" is dismissal. Listen for the exact phrase.
5. **Silence after the ask.** The single most powerful closing technique in any language. Make the ask, then stop talking. The first person to speak loses leverage.

## Hopkins's classic closes (still working in 2026)

These are formal closes — use them when the relationship and discovery have done their work. They're not magic; they're well-engineered language patterns.

- **The alternative close:** "Would you prefer to start in March or April?"
- **The assumptive close:** "Where would you like the implementation team to start?"
- **The minor-point close:** "And would you like the standard package or the premium one?"
- **The Ben Franklin close:** Pro/con list, drawn together with the buyer.
- **The sharp-angle close:** "If we could include training at no extra charge, would you be ready to sign today?"
- **The reduction to the ridiculous:** "That's $1,200 a month — or $40 a day — which is about the cost of two coffees and lunch."

None of these work if the discovery was weak. All of them work when discovery was strong.

## Puerto Rico application

### Closing pace

PR business culture **rarely closes in the first meeting** for considered purchases. Pushing for first-meeting close on B2B or high-ticket deals reads as pressure and often backfires. Expected pattern: first meeting = relationship + discovery, second meeting = proposal + first close attempt, third meeting (or WhatsApp follow-up) = decision. Exceptions: high-velocity B2C, automotive, urgent insurance, time-bound regulatory triggers — these compress normally.

### Closing language register

- **Spanish closes that work:** *"¿Arrancamos en marzo, o prefiere abril?"* / *"¿Firmamos hoy y empezamos el lunes?"* / *"Déjeme preguntarle directo — ¿qué falta para que esto sea un sí?"*
- **Closes to avoid:** anything that translates literally from aggressive English (*"¿Qué le impide firmar ahora mismo?"* sounds confrontational in PR; soften to *"¿Qué te hace falta saber para decidir?"*).
- **The *dueño* close:** when selling to family-business owners, name them. *"Don José, usted es quien decide — ¿vamos para adelante?"* The use of *don/doña* + name signals respect and clarifies the decision moment.

### Objection patterns specific to PR

- *"Lo voy a consultar con…"* (*"I'll consult with…"*) — often the spouse, the accountant, or a sibling. Not a stall — it's a real decision dynamic. Ask to be present for that conversation or to send written material structured for the absent decision-maker.
- *"Ahora no es el momento"* (*"Now isn't the moment"*) — explore whether it's truly timing, or it's a softened no. Label it: *"Parece que algo más está pesando — ¿qué es?"*
- *"Es mucho dinero"* (*"It's a lot of money"*) — reduce-to-the-ridiculous works (*"Sale a $X al día — ¿es el precio o es el valor?"*); also revisit the Value Equation.
- *"Tengo que pensarlo"* (*"I have to think about it"*) — the most common soft no. Probe: *"¿Qué específicamente quieres pensar?"* — forces a real objection to surface.

### Trust-based closes

For relationship-driven PR sales, **the verbal handshake matters more than the contract draft.** Get a clear, witnessed verbal yes from the *dueño* ("Dé por hecho que arrancamos") and contract-sign within 48 hours. Letting too much time pass between verbal commitment and paper signature is where PR deals most often die — not because the buyer reneges, but because life intervenes.
