# Strategic Selling
**Authors:** Robert B. Miller & Stephen E. Heiman  
**First published:** 1985  
**Section:** Classic Sales Techniques

## Overview
The foundational text of **complex / multi-stakeholder B2B selling**. Where SPIN deals with the conversation, Strategic Selling deals with the **deal**: the map of who's involved, what role each plays, where you stand with each, and what your next move is. It introduced the Blue Sheet (the deal-map artifact) that became standard at IBM, Procter & Gamble, and most enterprise sales orgs of the era.

## Author Context
Miller and Heiman were sales-effectiveness consultants who built their method by studying win/loss patterns at major enterprise sellers. The framework was acquired by Miller Heiman Group and is still used at Fortune 500 companies today.

## Key Concepts

### 1. The four buyer roles
Every complex deal has up to four roles, sometimes played by different people, sometimes one person playing two:
- **Economic Buyer** — final yes/no on the money. Only one per deal.
- **User Buyer(s)** — who will actually use the product day-to-day.
- **Technical Buyer(s)** — evaluates against specifications; can say no but not yes.
- **Coach** — someone inside the account who wants you to win and will tell you what's actually going on.

Missing any of the four is the most common loss-cause.

### 2. Response modes
Four mental states a buyer can be in:
- **Growth** — wants more, faster, bigger. Open to upside.
- **Trouble** — something is broken; wants it fixed.
- **Even Keel** — no change wanted; satisfied. Hardest to sell to.
- **Overconfident** — thinks they're winning when they're not. Closed to messaging until reality breaks through.

The seller diagnoses which mode each buyer is in and pitches accordingly.

### 3. Win-Results
A Win is a personal benefit the buyer receives. A Result is a measurable business outcome. Every buyer needs **both** to advance the deal. The seller's job is to identify each individual's Win and tie it to the business Result.

### 4. Red flags & strengths
Every account map has visible weaknesses (no Coach yet, uncontacted Economic Buyer, Technical Buyer in trouble mode) and strengths (multi-level relationships, strong coach, EB met). The Blue Sheet forces visibility.

## Why This Still Works in 2026
Enterprise buying committees have grown, not shrunk. Modern deals routinely involve 6–10 stakeholders. The Strategic Selling map is the original version of every modern "account-based selling" framework.

## Quotables
- "In a strategic sale, what matters is not what you do but how you think about what you do."
- "Don't accept business that doesn't yield positive win-results for everyone involved."
- "Information is the currency of strategic selling."

## Puerto Rico Application
- **Multi-stakeholder mapping is essential in PR enterprise / government deals**, where decision-making committees often blend professional and family-business dynamics.
- **Coach identification** is especially high-leverage — PR's tight professional networks mean a single well-placed Coach can transform deal trajectory.
- **Family-owned businesses** often blur the four roles (the EB might also be the User). The framework still applies — just expect role-merging more often than mainland deals.

## How to Use It
- AEs running deals > 6 weeks: build a Blue Sheet (or modern equivalent in Salesforce / HubSpot) for every active opportunity.
- Sales managers: deal-review meetings should be Blue Sheet walkthroughs, not pipeline status.
- Use alongside SPIN — Strategic Selling answers "who and what's the position," SPIN answers "what to ask once you're in the room."
