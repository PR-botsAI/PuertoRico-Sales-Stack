**Authors:** Chip Heath & Dan Heath  
**First Published:** 2007  
**Era:** Modern — Communication Science  
**Status:** Recommended by founders, billionaires, and political strategists alike — the manual for ideas that spread

## Why Top Performers Recommend This Book

Whether the audience is a single prospect or a million customers, the same six properties make an idea *stick*. The Heath brothers reverse-engineered decades of memorable advertising, urban legends, public-policy campaigns, and powerful speeches to extract a repeatable formula.

## The SUCCESs Framework

- **S — Simple:** Find the core. Most messages die from too much information.
- **U — Unexpected:** Break a pattern. Open a curiosity gap.
- **C — Concrete:** Specific, sensory detail beats abstract claims every time.
- **C — Credible:** Build trust through human-scale, verifiable details — not corporate jargon.
- **E — Emotional:** Make them *feel* before they think.
- **S — Stories:** Narratives encode information in the most memorable possible form.

## Application to Modern Sales

- **Pitch construction:** Apply SUCCESs to every deck slide, every elevator pitch, every cold email.
- **Curiosity gaps in subject lines:** "There's one thing we missed in your last quarter — may I show you?"
- **Human-scale credibility:** "This is what happened to a hospital exactly your size" beats "industry average" stats.
- **Stories as objection-handlers:** A 90-second customer story dissolves more objections than 9 minutes of feature explanation.

## Puerto Rico Market Relevance

PR culture is profoundly story-driven. *Cuentos* are the dominant communication form across generations and social classes. Sales reps who can package their pitch as a story — with a specific protagonist, a vivid setting, and an emotional payoff — outperform feature-pushers by enormous margins on the island. The Heath framework gives them an operating system for this skill.

## Key Takeaways

- Simple, unexpected, concrete, credible, emotional, story.
- Ideas don't spread because they're true. They spread because they're sticky.
- Specificity beats abstraction. Always.
