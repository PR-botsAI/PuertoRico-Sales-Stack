# Made to Stick
**Authors:** Chip Heath & Dan Heath  
**First published:** 2007  
**Section:** Billionaire & Top-Performer Recommendations

## Overview
Why do some ideas survive and others die? The Heath brothers (a Stanford organizational behavior professor and a Duke fellow) studied ideas that travel — from urban legends to viral campaigns to enduring religious teachings — and isolated six properties that consistently differentiate "sticky" ideas from forgettable ones. The result: a complete framework for crafting messages that lodge in memory and propagate on their own.

For sellers, this book is essential. The pitch that doesn't stick was effectively never delivered.

## Author Context
Chip Heath at Stanford GSB and Dan Heath at the Center for Strategic Communication at Duke have collaborated on a body of work bridging behavioral science and operational decision-making. Their later books (*Switch*, *Decisive*) extend the project.

## The Six Stickiness Properties: SUCCESs

### 1. Simple
Not "dumb-down" — **find the essential core.** The Heaths borrow the journalistic concept of the lead: the one thing that, if your reader takes away nothing else, is the thing they must remember. Strip ruthlessly until the core is undeniable.

The Southwest Airlines example: "We are THE low-fare airline." One core idea drives every downstream decision — no meals, no seat assignments, no hub-and-spoke. Strategic clarity through extreme simplicity.

### 2. Unexpected
The brain is wired to ignore the expected and attend to the surprising. Open with something that violates the listener's pattern. Then explain. The unresolved-curiosity gap is one of the strongest cognitive levers available.

*Example*: Nordstrom returning the customer's used tires — even though Nordstrom doesn't sell tires. The story propagates because it's pattern-breaking.

### 3. Concrete
Abstract ideas don't stick; concrete sensory details do. Specify the situation, the action, the outcome in tangible terms. JFK didn't say "let's lead the world in space"; he said "a man on the moon by the end of the decade." Different memorability category.

### 4. Credible
Claims need to feel checkable. Three kinds of credibility:
- **External** — expert citations, statistics.
- **Internal** — vivid details that imply the storyteller was there.
- **Testable** — invite the listener to verify ("Where were you in 1992 when …?").

### 5. Emotional
People care about people, not abstractions. The Mother Teresa principle: a single person's story moves donors more than statistics about millions. Find the individual face in the data.

### 6. Story
Narrative is the most cognitively privileged transmission format humans have. Three sticky story patterns:
- **Challenge plot** (underdog overcomes obstacle).
- **Connection plot** (people bridging differences).
- **Creativity plot** (someone solves a hard puzzle).

Wrap your message in one of these and stickiness multiplies.

## The Curse of Knowledge
The Heaths' diagnostic for why most expert communication fails. Once you know something, you cannot imagine not knowing it. The expert structures explanations around their own knowledge map, not the listener's. Sticky communication requires deliberately working against the curse: testing with naïve audiences, simplifying mercilessly, leading with concrete examples.

## Why This Still Works in 2026
The attention economy has made stickiness even more important. Marketing copy, pitch decks, ads, internal memos — all live or die by the SUCCESs properties. The book is one of the most-recommended communications texts in the YC Library and across major venture portfolios.

## Puerto Rico Application
- **PR communication is high-context and story-rich.** The SUCCESs framework operationalizes what good PR communicators do intuitively, making it teachable across teams.
- **Find the individual face** — PR marketing benefits enormously from real local customer stories rather than abstract benefit lists. The cultural register expects the personal.
- **Concreteness in Spanish:** sensory language lands stronger than corporate-translated abstractions. Build proof points around tangible PR-context details (cities, sectors, family dynamics).

## How to Use It
- Marketing teams: audit your current website and ads against SUCCESs. Most fail on Simple and Concrete.
- Sales enablement: rewrite the top-of-funnel pitch using the framework as a checklist.
- Founders: your investor narrative *must* hit at least four of the six. The deck that hits all six is the deck that gets funded.
