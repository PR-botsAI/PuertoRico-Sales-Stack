# Cold Calling for Chickens
*by Bob Etherington · 2006*

## Era & Classification
Mid-2000s practical cold-calling manual. Targeted at the seller who knows they need to prospect but freezes up at the dial-pad. A compact, technique-dense book — closer to a swimming-lesson handbook than a philosophical treatise.

## Overview
Etherington wrote this for the introverted, the new-to-sales, and the experienced-but-call-reluctant. The thesis: cold calling is a *learnable mechanical skill*, not a personality trait. Sellers who think they're "not the cold-calling type" have simply never been taught the mechanics — opening, qualifying, handling brush-offs, booking the meeting — in a format short enough to practice and direct enough to apply.

The "chickens" in the title isn't an insult. It's recognition that fear of rejection is the universal seller bottleneck, and the book treats it directly.

## Core Premise
Cold-call success is 80% mechanics, 20% mindset. Master the mechanics and the mindset follows. Sellers who try to fix mindset first — through motivation, affirmations, or psyching themselves up — never get to the technique that actually produces meetings.

## Key Concepts

### The Opening 15 Seconds
Most cold calls are won or lost in the first 15 seconds. Etherington's opening structure:
1. **Name + company** delivered with peer-level confidence.
2. **Reason for the call** in one sentence, tied to the prospect's likely current concern.
3. **Permission ask** — *"is now a bad moment?"* (Permission to continue is more powerful than apology for interrupting.)
4. **The hook** — a quantified outcome statement that earns the next 30 seconds.

### The Permission Reversal
*"Is now a bad moment?"* outperforms *"Do you have a minute?"* by 3-4x. The first acknowledges the prospect's autonomy and triggers a polite *no, it's fine* response. The second triggers a defensive *no, I don't*.

### Brush-Off Handling
Etherington catalogs the seven most common brush-offs ("not interested," "send me information," "we already have a provider," etc.) and provides a script-template response for each. The pattern: acknowledge the brush-off, reframe with a quick insight, ask a single qualifying question, route to the meeting ask.

Example — "We already have a provider":
> *That's actually what I expected — most of the leaders we work with did when we first spoke. They moved forward not because their current setup was broken, but because what they were missing was costing them quarterly. Worth a 15-minute look at what we're seeing this quarter?*

### The Single Objective
Every cold call has exactly one objective: book the meeting. Not educate, not pitch, not handle objections in detail. Sellers who try to close on the call lose the meeting they could have had.

### The Math of the Phone
Etherington reminds sellers that cold calling is a numbers game with stable conversion ratios. A typical pattern: 100 dials → 20-30 conversations → 5-8 meetings booked → 1-2 deals. Once you accept the math, individual rejections stop mattering.

## Signature Frameworks

### The 4-Step Cold Call
1. **Open** — name, company, permission reversal.
2. **Hook** — quantified peer outcome.
3. **Qualify** — one or two diagnostic questions.
4. **Close for meeting** — specific time, two options.

### The 3-Touch Voicemail Sequence
For unreachable prospects: voicemail #1 (peer-level outcome), email follow-up #1 (same outcome restated, single ask), voicemail #2 (different angle, slightly stronger urgency), email #2 (different angle), voicemail #3 (final, with explicit break-up frame). The full sequence runs 10-14 days, then the prospect is paused and re-queued in 60-90 days.

### The Recording Drill
Record your own calls. Listen back. Cringe at the verbal tics. Eliminate them one at a time over weeks. Most sellers refuse to do this; the ones who do close measurably more.

## Quotables

> *Cold calling is mechanics. Mindset follows mechanics, not the other way around.*
> *The first 15 seconds buys you the next 30.*
> *Your only job on the call is to book the meeting.*
> *"Is now a bad moment?" beats "Do you have a minute?" every time.*

## Why Top Performers Cite It
Cold-call coaching programs across SaaS, financial services, and B2B services treat this book as required reading for new SDRs and BDRs. The permission-reversal opener and brush-off scripts have been borrowed into virtually every modern sales-training curriculum, often without attribution. It's a short book, intentionally — meant to be re-read every quarter.

## Application in the Puerto Rico Industry
- **Cold calling in PR works best bilingually.** The opener delivered in English signals mainland-vendor; the same opener delivered in Spanish signals local-peer. Sellers who can switch based on who picks up the phone close 2-3x more meetings.
- **The permission reversal lands extra hard in PR culture**, where courtesy is highly valued. *"¿Es mal momento?"* is a culturally native opener.
- **The math holds but the ratios shift.** PR's smaller TAM means dial volumes are lower per territory, but conversation-to-meeting conversion is higher (warmer audience, less call fatigue than US markets). A PR SDR doing 30-40 quality dials per day produces what a US SDR produces in 80-100.
- **Recorded-call self-review is rare on the island and high-leverage.** Sellers who adopt it differentiate quickly.

## How to Study This Book
1. Read in one sitting (under 3 hours).
2. Write your own opener using the 4-step structure. Bilingual versions.
3. Make 20 calls per day for 10 days using the new framework. Track conversion.
4. Record 5 calls per week. Review one with a peer or manager. Adjust.
5. Pair with *Fanatical Prospecting* (Blount) for the pipeline-management layer.
