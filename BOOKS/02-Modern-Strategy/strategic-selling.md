# Strategic Selling
*by Robert B. Miller & Stephen E. Heiman · 1985 (revised editions through 2022)*

## Era & Classification
1980s enterprise-sales methodology. The first widely-taught framework that treated complex B2B deals as multi-stakeholder strategic projects rather than one-on-one persuasion exercises. Still the spine of most enterprise sales training programs four decades later.

## Overview
Miller and Heiman developed the Strategic Selling methodology while working with sales teams on multi-million-dollar industrial accounts. Their insight: in any deal larger than one buyer can authorize alone, you're not selling to a person — you're selling through a web of buying influences, each with their own role, reaction mode, and degree of influence.

The Blue Sheet — the methodology's signature artifact — is a one-page deal map that forces the seller to identify every buying influence, classify their reaction, and plan specific actions to move the deal forward.

## Core Premise
Complex sales are won and lost based on the seller's coverage of all relevant buying influences, not on the strength of any single relationship. A deal where you have one champion but three unmapped detractors is a deal you will lose. The Blue Sheet exists to make the invisible map of stakeholders visible.

## Key Concepts

### The Four Buying Influences
Every complex deal has four categories of stakeholder, often filled by different people (sometimes by the same person wearing multiple hats):

1. **Economic Buyer (EB)** — Controls the money. Has veto power. There is exactly one Economic Buyer per deal, even when a committee buys.
2. **User Buyer (UB)** — Will personally use or be affected by your solution. Multiple per deal.
3. **Technical Buyer (TB)** — Evaluates against specifications and standards. Cannot say final yes, but can say final no. Multiple per deal (legal, IT security, procurement).
4. **Coach** — Internal advocate who provides intelligence on the buying process. The Coach must benefit personally from your win, must be credible to other buyers, and must want you to win.

### The Four Reaction Modes
Each buying influence is in one of four mental states about your offer:

1. **Growth (G)** — Wants more/better; sees your solution as a path to growth.
2. **Trouble (T)** — Has a problem; sees your solution as a fix.
3. **Even Keel (EK)** — Believes everything is fine; sees no need for change.
4. **Overconfident (OC)** — Believes things are *better* than fine; actively rejects change.

**Sellable modes: G and T. Unsellable until shifted: EK and OC.**

### The Win-Win Matrix
Every deal has four possible outcomes by stakeholder: you win and they win (✓✓), you win and they lose (✓✗), you lose and they win (✗✓), or you both lose (✗✗). Only ✓✓ outcomes produce repeat business, referrals, and account growth. The methodology forces the seller to design specifically for ✓✓ across all buying influences.

### Red Flags
Miller-Heiman taught sellers to deliberately surface what's *wrong* with a deal. Red flags include: missing information, an uncovered buying influence, an EK or OC mode unaddressed, weak Coach, or an upcoming change in personnel. The Blue Sheet exists in part to make red flags impossible to ignore.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Blue Sheet
A one-page strategic-account planning tool. Sections: buying influences (named individuals in each category), reaction mode for each, degree of influence, status ("+" for support, "-" for opposition, "?" for unknown), and specific next actions per influence. The Blue Sheet is updated weekly for active deals.

### The Single Sales Objective (SSO)
Define exactly what you're selling in this specific deal cycle. Not "more business with Acme Corp" but "sell the enterprise license, 500 seats, contract signed by Q3 close, $2.4M ACV." Concrete enough that you can tell whether you won.

### Strategic vs. Tactical Selling
Strategy answers "who, what, when" — which influences, what objective, what timeline. Tactics answer "how" — what to say in the next meeting. The methodology insists strategy must come before tactics. Most sellers reverse this and lose.

## Why Top Performers Cite It
Strategic Selling is the foundational methodology behind Miller Heiman Group (now part of Korn Ferry), which trains the enterprise sales orgs of most Fortune 500 companies. Every modern enterprise framework — MEDDIC, MEDDPICC, Command of the Message, Challenger — builds on or reacts to the buying-influences map first popularized by Miller-Heiman.

## Application in the Puerto Rico Industry
- **Government and large-corporate deals in PR are explicitly multi-stakeholder.** Selling to PREPA, government agencies, hospital systems, or the major banks involves 5-12 buying influences with conflicting incentives. Strategic Selling is the only methodology built for this complexity.
- **The Coach concept is amplified in PR's relationship economy.** A well-placed Coach inside a target account — often a former colleague or industry connection — is the single highest-leverage asset in PR enterprise selling.
- **Reaction modes shift slower in PR culture.** Even Keel and Overconfident influences require longer education cycles. The methodology's discipline of *not pitching* until reaction mode is sellable saves quarters of wasted effort.
- **Local TBs (Technical Buyers) are gate-keepers with veto power and long memory.** Procurement, IT, and legal in PR institutions often have 15-20 year tenure and remember every vendor who tried to circumvent them.

## How to Study This Book
1. Read the book once for theory.
2. Print a Blue Sheet template. Fill it out for your three largest open deals.
3. Use the Blue Sheet weekly for 90 days. Most sellers feel the methodology click around week 6.
4. Pair with *The Challenger Sale* for the value-creation layer that complements the mapping layer.
