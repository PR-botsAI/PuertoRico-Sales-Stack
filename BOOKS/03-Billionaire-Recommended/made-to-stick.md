# Made to Stick — Chip Heath & Dan Heath

## Bibliographic

- **Title:** Made to Stick: Why Some Ideas Survive and Others Die
- **Authors:** Chip Heath (Stanford Graduate School of Business) and Dan Heath (Duke CASE Center)
- **First published:** 2007, Random House
- **Genre:** Communication / behavioral science / marketing
- **Length:** ~290 pages
- **Era:** Era 3 — Modern Acquisition (communication science)
- **Why it matters for sales:** Sales is the transfer of belief through language. Most pitches fail because the *idea* doesn't survive past the meeting, not because the product is bad. The Heaths reverse-engineered what makes ideas stick — urban legends, proverbs, conspiracy theories, great political slogans — and turned it into a teachable checklist. For sellers, this is the single best book on how to construct a message a prospect can repeat to their boss after you leave the room.

## Core Thesis

Ideas that stick share six measurable traits, captured in the acronym **SUCCESs**: **Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories**. None of these traits is innate to the idea — they are choices the communicator makes during framing. The default mode of expert communicators is the opposite of sticky: abstract, jargon-loaded, statistic-driven, emotionally flat. The Heaths call this the **Curse of Knowledge** — once you know something deeply, you lose the ability to imagine not knowing it, and you systematically over-explain in ways that lose the listener.

For sales: your product is *your* expertise. The prospect is the non-expert. Every pitch is a fight against the Curse of Knowledge. SUCCESs is the antidote.

## Key Frameworks

### The SUCCESs Checklist

**S — Simple.** Find the core. Not dumbed-down, not a summary — the single most important truth, expressed as compactly as possible. The Heaths cite Southwest Airlines' "THE low-fare airline" as the prototype: every operational decision is filtered through that one phrase. A simple core acts as a forcing function — when employees, partners, or prospects face ambiguity, they can resolve it without asking.

**U — Unexpected.** Break a pattern. Generate surprise to open the gate of attention, then generate interest (a curiosity gap) to keep it open. The "curiosity gap" — a deliberate hole in the listener's knowledge that they feel compelled to fill — is the engine of sustained attention. Cliffhangers, surprising statistics, counterintuitive openers.

**C — Concrete.** Speak in things people can see, hear, touch, taste. "World-class customer service" is abstract; "we will mail you a replacement before you ship the broken one back" is concrete. Concrete language is the bridge between expert and novice — both can see the same picture.

**C — Credible.** Sticky ideas carry their own credentials. External authority (experts, statistics) works but is weak. Internal credibility — vivid details, named sources, testable claims, the "Sinatra test" (one perfect reference that ends the conversation) — works better. The strongest form is the **testable credential**: "Try it for yourself."

**E — Emotional.** People care about people, not abstractions. The famous research the Heaths cite: donors gave dramatically more when shown a single starving girl named Rokia than when shown statistics about millions of starving children — and gave *less* when shown both, because the statistics activated analytical thinking and shut down empathy. Emotion is not a garnish; it is the carrier wave.

**S — Stories.** Stories are flight simulators for the mind. They embed the lesson in a sequence the listener can replay. The Heaths identify three story plots that recur in sticky ideas: **Challenge** (David vs. Goliath), **Connection** (bridging a gap between people), and **Creativity** (solving a problem in a novel way).

### The Curse of Knowledge

The book's central villain. Once you know something — your product, your industry, the answer to a problem — you cannot un-know it, and you systematically overestimate how obvious it is to others. The Heaths reference the classic "tappers and listeners" experiment: tappers tap out the rhythm of a well-known song, listeners try to identify it. Tappers predict 50% success; actual rate is ~2.5%. The tapper hears the melody in their head; the listener hears only taps. Every salesperson who has ever said "but the value is obvious" has been a tapper.

### The Commander's Intent

Borrowed from the US Army. Before any operation, the commander states the single most important outcome in plain language — the "commander's intent" — separate from the detailed plan. When the plan inevitably breaks (which it always does), every soldier still knows what to optimize for. In sales: every account, every campaign, every quarter should have a commander's intent. "Close three deals over $50K" is one. "Become the standard tool inside the customer's design team" is another. The plan changes; the intent doesn't.

## Principles & Tactics

1. **Lead the burying.** Journalists call it "burying the lede" — putting the most important fact deep in the article. Salespeople do this constantly: the discount, the differentiator, the proof point ends up on slide 14. Move the lede to the first sentence.

2. **Force prioritization.** "If you say three things, you say nothing." Make yourself pick the one fact that, if the prospect remembered nothing else, would be enough. Build everything around it.

3. **Use proverbs.** A proverb is the maximum compression of an idea: simple, concrete, emotional, and memorable enough to survive being repeated. "Names, names, and names" (the journalist Dan Heath's father's rule for local newspapers) is a proverb. Build proverbs into your messaging. "We're the cheapest cloud storage on Earth" is a proverb. "We provide enterprise-grade hyperscale solutions" is not.

4. **Generate curiosity gaps before you fill them.** Open with the mystery, not the answer. "Why does our biggest competitor refuse to publish their numbers?" pulls the prospect into the conversation; "Our software is faster than X" pushes them away.

5. **Avoid the abstraction ladder trap.** When you can't get traction, climb *down* the ladder — get more concrete, not more abstract. Most sellers do the opposite, retreating into bigger and more impressive-sounding categories when the buyer disengages.

6. **Use the Sinatra Test.** "If I can make it there, I can make it anywhere." One unimpeachable reference can replace ten weak ones. Find your Sinatra customer for each prospect segment and lead with that name.

7. **Anchor in the prospect's existing schema.** Velcro models — connect every new claim to something the prospect already knows. Don't introduce three unfamiliar concepts at once. Build one bridge, then walk across.

8. **Tell case stories, not case studies.** Case studies are PDFs. Case stories are oral, character-driven, conflict-driven, repeatable. The prospect should be able to retell your case story to their CEO without notes.

## Notable Concepts

- **Tappers and Listeners** — the cognitive asymmetry between someone who knows and someone who doesn't
- **Curse of Knowledge** — the universal expert tax on communication
- **SUCCESs** — six-trait checklist for sticky ideas
- **Commander's Intent** — the priority-anchoring discipline
- **The Sinatra Test** — one perfect reference as proof
- **Curiosity Gap** — Loewenstein's information-gap theory of curiosity applied to messaging
- **Inversion** — sticky ideas often invert what listeners expect (Southwest's "no frills" inverted airline category norms)
- **Generative Analogies** — analogies that don't just describe but generate new ideas (Disney's "cast members" instead of employees)
- **Identity Appeals** — "people like us do things like this"; tying behavior to identity rather than incentive

## Critique & Context

The book is rooted in social-science research on memory, attention, and persuasion (heavy debt to Cialdini, Loewenstein on curiosity, Slovic on identifiable-victim effect). Its weakness, as some marketers note, is that the framework is descriptive — it explains why ideas stick after the fact — and applying it prospectively to design a sticky message requires creative judgment the book can't fully systematize. The Heaths acknowledge this in the closing chapters.

Critics also note that "stickiness" optimized for memorability can drift toward manipulation if untethered from truth. The Heaths address this briefly but the warning is worth repeating: a sticky lie sticks too. The book pairs well with Cialdini's *Influence* (which adds the ethical-influence frame) and with Pink's *To Sell Is Human* (which adds the serving frame).

In sales literature, *Made to Stick* sits in a peculiar position: it's not a sales book, but every modern sales-enablement program — pitch decks, narrative frameworks, "challenger" messaging — borrows from it. Aaron Ross's *Predictable Revenue* uses SUCCESs principles in its outbound email templates. The Challenger Sale's "Teach" pillar leans on Unexpected + Concrete. Hormozi's offer-construction language is concrete-emotional in textbook Heath fashion.

The book is short, dense, and rereadable. Most sellers benefit from one full read, then quarterly skims of specific chapters when preparing for major pitches.

## Puerto Rico Application

**Bilingual stickiness.** Spanish and English have different rhythmic and rhetorical norms. A proverb that works in English ("We're the cheapest cloud storage on Earth") may not survive translation. Puerto Rican audiences respond strongly to Spanish proverbs and refranes — "el que no llora, no mama," "más vale pájaro en mano" — and a seller fluent in both languages can build proverb-strength taglines in either tongue. Test each version with native speakers, not Google Translate. A sticky message in one language is often only adequate in the other.

**Concrete over abstract is doubly important.** Puerto Rican business culture tends toward warm, narrative, person-centered communication. Abstract corporate-speak ("synergistic enterprise-grade solutions") lands flat in San Juan boardrooms in a way it might not in midtown Manhattan. Lead with the picture, the name, the place. "We're the same system Ferreterías Méndez uses in Bayamón" beats "we serve mid-market retail."

**Stories carry weight in confianza-based markets.** When trust must be earned before any deal, a well-told case story does triple duty: it demonstrates competence, builds rapport, and gives the prospect something to repeat to their family or partners — who often weigh in on B2B decisions on the island. Build case stories around local prospects whenever possible. A Puerto Rican prospect will retell a story about another Puerto Rican company; they may quietly discount a Texas case study.

**The Curse of Knowledge in regulated industries.** Insurance, healthcare, and financial services on the island are weighed down by acronyms and federal-state regulatory overlap (ASES, ASSMCA, OCIF, Ley 60). Sellers in these spaces routinely violate every SUCCESs principle by accident, drowning prospects in jargon. Translate every technical term into a concrete consequence. Don't say "our platform is HIPAA-compliant and integrates with ASES requirements"; say "if a patient files a complaint, you can pull up every interaction in under 30 seconds — the auditor leaves happy."

**Emotional stakes are higher in family businesses.** Many PR companies are second- or third-generation family operations. The buyer isn't just a buyer; they're a steward of their parents' or grandparents' work. Pitches that reduce decisions to ROI miss the deeper stake. Emotional framing — legacy, family, continuity — is not manipulation in this context; it's accuracy.

**Curiosity gaps in cold outreach.** Email open rates on the island skew higher than US averages when subject lines are in Spanish and contain a specific local detail. A subject line like "Lo que vimos en su sucursal de Caguas" (concrete + curiosity gap + unexpected — "wait, you visited?") will outperform "Quick question" by a factor of 5–10x in PR markets. Build curiosity gaps using local geography, local names, local recent events.

## Reading Priority

**Priority: Tier 1 — Read in first 90 days.**

This is the foundational communication book. Every other sales book you read will be easier to apply if you've internalized SUCCESs first, because most sales books *implicitly* use these principles in their examples without naming them. Reading *Made to Stick* gives you the meta-language to dissect any pitch deck, email, or sales script you encounter.

**Suggested companion reads:**
- Cialdini, *Influence* — for the ethical guardrails on persuasion
- Pink, *To Sell Is Human* — for the modern frame of selling as serving
- Klaff, *Pitch Anything* — for stagecraft and frame control built on similar principles
- Heath & Heath, *Switch* (their later book) — for applying these ideas to change management, useful for enterprise sellers
