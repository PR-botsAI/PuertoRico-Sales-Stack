# 04 — Generational Profiles

*Every generation buys differently. Channel, pace, proof type, language, and decision style all shift across cohorts. Sell with the wrong generational register and the deal closes for someone else.*

## Why this lens matters

A Boomer reads email and trusts handshakes. A Gen X-er Googles you and trusts referrals. A Millennial DMs you and trusts reviews. A Gen Z-er finds you on TikTok and trusts creators they follow. Same product, four entirely different sales motions.

This isn't about stereotyping — it's about defaults. Plenty of 65-year-olds use TikTok and plenty of 25-year-olds prefer phone calls. The point is **calibrate the default and let the buyer correct you**, not the reverse.

## The Boomer (1946–1964, ages 60–79 in 2026)

### Defaults
- Phone and in-person are preferred channels
- Email is acceptable; LinkedIn is fine; social media for personal use, not business
- Values: loyalty, expertise, experience, established institutions
- Distrusts: hype, jargon, surface-only digital sellers, anything that feels like a scheme
- Buys: from people they trust; relationship precedes transaction
- Decides: thoughtfully, often after consulting spouse and trusted advisors

### What they buy at this life stage
- Healthcare and long-term care planning
- Retirement income vehicles, annuities, conservative investments
- Travel (often premium; this is the cohort with the most discretionary travel budget right now)
- Home renovations (especially aging-in-place)
- Estate planning and wealth transfer to children/grandchildren
- Quality goods ("I'd rather pay once and buy right")
- Gifts for grandchildren
- Healthcare-adjacent products: mobility, hearing, vision

### How to sell to Boomers
- **Phone first, in-person whenever possible.** Email to follow up, not to lead.
- **Slow the pace.** Multiple touchpoints, not single-call close.
- **Show your track record and credentials.** Years in business, certifications, reputable references.
- **Print materials still matter.** A well-designed printed proposal or brochure carries weight.
- **Address spouses together.** Joint decisions are the norm.
- **Respect their experience.** They've been pitched for 50 years; condescension is a hard no.
- **Use formal language and titles.** "Mr." / "Mrs." / *Don/Doña* until invited otherwise.
- **Pay attention to grandchildren.** Many Boomer purchases are motivated by grandkids more than themselves.

### Channel summary
Phone (high) > In-person (high) > Email (medium) > Direct mail (medium) > Facebook (medium for personal; low for sales) > TV/radio (medium) > LinkedIn (medium) > Instagram/TikTok (low)

## Generation X (1965–1980, ages 44–59 in 2026)

### Defaults
- Email is the default professional channel; phone is acceptable for relationship moments
- Texts and WhatsApp for personal and quick business
- Skeptical, independent, self-researching
- Values: efficiency, honesty, expertise, no-BS communication
- Distrusts: hype, marketing fluff, anything that feels manipulative
- Buys: after research; verifies claims; reads reviews; checks references
- Decides: independently or as a couple; doesn't need extensive committee input

### What they buy at this life stage
- Career-defining services (executive coaching, MBAs, advanced certifications)
- Children's education investments (college funds, private school, tutoring, sports)
- Mid-tier-to-premium home upgrades
- Health and longevity products (this is the cohort discovering their body's limits)
- Wealth management and tax planning (peak earning years)
- Travel (sandwiched between aging parents and growing kids — they value vacations highly)
- Tools that save time (Gen X is overcommitted; time-saving is real value)

### How to sell to Gen X
- **Lead with substance.** They will tune out fluff in 30 seconds.
- **Email is preferred for the initial reach.** Follow with phone or WhatsApp.
- **Be honest about tradeoffs.** Don't overclaim. They respect a seller who admits limits.
- **Provide reviews and case studies.** They will Google you before responding; have a clean digital presence.
- **Respect their time.** Stay on schedule. End meetings on time.
- **Use data and references in equal measure.** They want both proof types.
- **Don't condescend or hype.** Match their level of intelligence (which is high).

### Channel summary
Email (high) > Phone (high) > WhatsApp/text (high) > LinkedIn (high) > In-person (medium-high) > Facebook (medium) > Instagram (medium) > TikTok (low) > TV (low) > Direct mail (low)

## Millennials (1981–1996, ages 28–43 in 2026)

### Defaults
- Text/DM/WhatsApp is preferred; phone calls require scheduling first
- Email is acceptable but they read it fast and on phones
- Heavy social media use across platforms
- Values: authenticity, transparency, social impact, experience over things
- Distrusts: corporate-speak, hidden fees, lack of online reviews, anything that feels fake
- Buys: experiences over goods; subscriptions over ownership; values-aligned brands
- Decides: after extensive online research; checks Instagram, Reddit, TikTok, Google reviews

### What they buy at this life stage
- First or second home (housing is delayed for this cohort, but happening now)
- Wedding-related products (delayed marriage cohort; still happening)
- Childcare and early education (peak years for new parents)
- Subscription services across categories (SaaS, streaming, fitness, meal kits)
- Travel (experience-focused; Instagram-worthy destinations)
- Health and wellness (mental health is huge for this cohort)
- Professional development
- Side-hustle and entrepreneurship tools
- Tech-forward financial products (Robinhood-style apps, crypto exposure, modern banking)

### How to sell to Millennials
- **Have a clean digital presence.** Website, Google reviews, social media — if any of these are weak, you're out.
- **Lead with values and authenticity.** Why do you do this? What do you stand for?
- **DM and text work.** Phone calls feel intrusive without scheduling.
- **Provide social proof at scale.** Reviews, ratings, testimonials, UGC, before/afters.
- **Be transparent about pricing.** Hidden fees are deal-breakers.
- **Mobile-first proposals.** They read on phones; design accordingly.
- **Subscription/monthly framing works well.** Ownership models can feel heavy.
- **Include sustainability/impact if relevant.** Don't fake it; if you have a real impact story, lead with it.

### Channel summary
Instagram (high) > WhatsApp/text (high) > Email (medium-high, on mobile) > TikTok (medium-high and rising) > LinkedIn (medium-high for B2B) > Google reviews (decisive) > Phone (medium, scheduled only) > In-person (medium) > Facebook (low and declining)

## Generation Z (1997–2012, ages 14–29 in 2026)

### Defaults
- Short-form video native: TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts
- DMs > texts > email; email feels formal/cold to many in this cohort
- Heavy use of recommendation algorithms; trust creators more than brands
- Values: authenticity, mental health, social/environmental impact, individuality, financial literacy (their post-pandemic cohort is unusually money-conscious)
- Distrusts: marketing performance, corporate stiffness, anything that feels boomer-coded
- Buys: based on creator recommendations, community trust, micro-trends
- Decides: faster than older cohorts on small purchases; slower on big ones (financially cautious)

### What they buy at this life stage
- Education (still in college or graduate school for many; trade-school resurgent)
- First professional clothing and gear
- First car (many delayed; some never)
- Tech and gadgets
- Wellness products (mental health, sleep, fitness, supplements)
- Travel (different mode than Millennials — less Instagram, more local/authentic)
- Creator-economy tools (cameras, editing software, microphones, online courses)
- Investment apps and crypto (peak demographic for these)
- Side-hustle and freelance tools

### How to sell to Gen Z
- **Short-form video is the entry channel.** Not optional for most consumer categories.
- **Creator partnerships outperform paid ads.** Micro-influencers (5K–50K followers) often outperform celebrity.
- **Show real, behind-the-scenes content.** Polished commercial-style ads underperform unpolished authentic content.
- **Make purchase frictionless.** Multiple-step checkouts, login walls, account-creation requirements — each step drops conversion.
- **Mobile only.** If your site or product doesn't work cleanly on mobile, you don't exist.
- **DM customer service responsively.** Slow customer service is a brand-killer for this cohort.
- **Be on the platforms they're on, not where you wish they were.**
- **Honest pricing, honest about flaws.** They'll find and post the flaws if you hide them.

### Channel summary
TikTok (high) > Instagram (high) > YouTube Shorts (high) > Discord (medium-high for community) > Twitch (medium for gaming/tech) > WhatsApp/DMs (high for communication) > LinkedIn (medium for the older end) > Email (medium-low) > Facebook (very low) > TV/radio (very low) > Phone calls (low)

## Cross-generational patterns to remember

- **Decision-speed inverts with age** for many purchase categories. Younger buyers decide faster on small purchases (TikTok-to-checkout in minutes); older buyers decide faster on big purchases (cash-rich Boomer can buy a house this week; cash-poor Gen Z takes 6 months).
- **Trust signals invert with age.** Boomers trust institutions; Gen Z trusts individuals (creators). Gen X and Millennials split.
- **Proof types differ.** Boomers want credentials; Gen X wants references; Millennials want reviews; Gen Z wants creator endorsement.
- **Family involvement varies.** Boomers consult spouse; Gen X decides independently; Millennials consult friends and online communities; Gen Z consults creators and TikTok.

## Puerto Rico calibration

Generational profiles in PR are similar to mainland defaults *plus* an additional cultural overlay:

- **PR Boomers** are even more relationship-driven than mainland Boomers. The phone-first / in-person preference is strong, plus Spanish-language defaults are nearly universal. *Don/Doña* + *usted* register is essential. WhatsApp use is high in this cohort — they adapted to it for family communication and now use it for business as well. Don't underestimate Boomer WhatsApp adoption in PR — it's much higher than the equivalent in the mainland US.
- **PR Gen X** is the bilingual bridge generation. Comfortable in both languages; often Spanish-default at home, English-comfortable at work. They drive most of the family-business operational decisions today. WhatsApp Business is their primary outreach channel.
- **PR Millennials** are heavily diaspora-connected — most have siblings, cousins, or close friends on the mainland. International references resonate. Spanish-language content is preferred, but mainland-quality production is expected. They use Instagram heavily and TikTok increasingly.
- **PR Gen Z** is the most digitally native cohort the island has ever had. TikTok in Spanish is dominant; their world is creator-mediated; many are reconnecting with *boricua* identity through digital content. This cohort drives Spanish-language content demand. They are also the most price-sensitive due to economic conditions and student debt.
