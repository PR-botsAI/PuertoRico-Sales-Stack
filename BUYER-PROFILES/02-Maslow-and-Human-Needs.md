# 02 — Maslow & The Six Human Needs

*What level of human need is your offer addressing? If you're selling at the wrong level for this buyer, you'll lose the deal even if your product is perfect.*

## Why this lens matters

Maslow gave us the classic hierarchy: physiological → safety → belonging → esteem → self-actualization. Tony Robbins refined it for sales/persuasion practice into Six Human Needs. Together they form the most reliable framework for *why* a buyer wants what they want — underneath the surface reason they give you.

Most sellers pitch features. Better sellers pitch benefits. Elite sellers pitch to the *human need* the buyer is unconsciously trying to satisfy. That's the unlock.

## Maslow's hierarchy — the sales-applied version

### Level 1 — Physiological
Food, water, shelter, sleep, basic health. Buyers in survival mode are not buying aspiration; they're buying *next meal, next month's rent, this prescription*. Any pitch above this level lands as irrelevant.

**Sell here:** essentials with dignity; cooperative pricing; access to basics. **Don’t sell here:** luxury, aspiration, anything that requires the buyer to have already moved up.

### Level 2 — Safety
Financial security, health insurance, job security, physical safety, predictability. This is the *biggest* selling level for the middle class — most of what they buy is some form of risk reduction.

**Sell here:** insurance, savings products, locks/security/cameras, established brands, warranties, money-back guarantees, retirement vehicles, healthcare, stable employment offers.

### Level 3 — Belonging & Love
Family, friendships, community, romantic partnership, identity in a group. Powerful driver across all wealth tiers but especially strong in PR's family-centered culture.

**Sell here:** anything that connects them to family or community — gifts, shared experiences, club memberships, family vacations, group products, religious products, gathering venues, dating services.

### Level 4 — Esteem
Recognition, respect, status, achievement, mastery, self-respect. The dominant selling level for upper-middle and lower-affluent buyers. They've solved safety; they want to be seen.

**Sell here:** awards, credentials, premium brands, executive education, status memberships, designation products (the watch, the car, the school).

### Level 5 — Self-Actualization
Meaning, purpose, mastery, contribution, creativity, peak experience. The dominant selling level for the affluent (who've solved everything below) and a meaningful minority of professionals seeking purpose-driven work.

**Sell here:** legacy products, philanthropy, mastery-pursuit experiences, transformational programs, creative pursuits, mentor-and-be-mentored opportunities, contribution platforms.

## Robbins's Six Human Needs

Refinement of Maslow that's more directly useful for sales practice. Every buyer prioritizes the six differently. Identify the buyer's top 2 and lead with them.

### 1. Certainty
Need for predictability, security, comfort, stability. Avoidance of risk, surprise, change. Strongest in older buyers, post-trauma buyers, and conservative cultures.

**Buyer signals:** asks about guarantees, asks about "how long you've been doing this," wants references, hesitates on novel solutions, asks about risk repeatedly.

**Sell to certainty:** warranties, established brands, money-back guarantees, long track record, conservative claims, slow rollout, pilots, references.

### 2. Variety / Uncertainty
Need for novelty, change, surprise, adventure, stimulation. The opposite of certainty. Strongest in younger buyers, creative professionals, restless types.

**Buyer signals:** asks "what's new," gets bored quickly, wants premium/exclusive/limited editions, is drawn to novelty, talks about travel and experiences.

**Sell to variety:** new launches, limited editions, first-access positioning, customization, novel features, surprise bonuses.

### 3. Significance
Need to feel important, unique, special, seen, recognized. Status-driven need; strong in entrepreneurs, performers, and many young professionals.

**Buyer signals:** name-drops, talks about position/title, wants the "executive" or "premium" tier, responds to recognition, sensitive to perceived disrespect.

**Sell to significance:** VIP treatment, named/personalized service, public recognition, exclusivity, premium tiers, "by invitation," status symbols.

### 4. Love / Connection
Need for closeness, intimacy, belonging, community, family. The opposite of significance (which is being elevated above others). Strongest in family-oriented cultures — including PR.

**Buyer signals:** talks about family/spouse/kids in opening minutes; warm in conversation; values long relationships; references community; uses "we" language.

**Sell to connection:** family-shared experiences, community memberships, gift-giving framing, multi-generational products, anything that strengthens existing relationships.

### 5. Growth
Need to develop, learn, improve, become more capable. Strong in ambitious professionals and lifelong learners.

**Buyer signals:** reads a lot, asks about skill development, takes courses, talks about goals and progress, frames decisions around learning.

**Sell to growth:** education, coaching, certifications, training, mastery programs, skill-development products, books, courses, mentorship access.

### 6. Contribution
Need to give back, make a difference, leave a legacy, serve something bigger than self. Strongest in mature buyers and the affluent who've satisfied lower needs.

**Buyer signals:** talks about impact, philanthropy, mentoring, family legacy, making a difference; values purpose; asks about social/environmental dimensions.

**Sell to contribution:** legacy products, philanthropic vehicles, mentorship opportunities, impact-aligned products, named-foundation services, products with charitable components.

## The diagnostic conversation

You can identify a buyer's top two needs within five minutes of conversation. Ask:

1. **"What's most important to you in solving X?"** (Open answer; listen for the value-word that recurs.)
2. **"Tell me about a time this worked well for you."** (Past patterns reveal current priorities.)
3. **"What would success look like in 12 months?"** (Future vision reveals the active need.)

Then listen for **the words that signal each need:**

| Buyer says… | Top need likely is… |
|---|---|
| "secure," "reliable," "proven," "safe," "guaranteed" | Certainty |
| "exciting," "new," "different," "unique," "latest" | Variety |
| "the best," "premier," "top-tier," "executive," "VIP" | Significance |
| "family," "my team," "we," "together," "share" | Connection |
| "learn," "improve," "better," "develop," "master" | Growth |
| "impact," "give back," "matter," "legacy," "purpose" | Contribution |

## Combining lenses — the wealth-class × needs matrix

When you combine the [wealth-class lens](./01-Wealth-Class-Segmentation.md) with the Six Human Needs, patterns emerge:

- **Affluent + Significance** → the status-driven wealthy buyer. Sell exclusivity, named-product, by-invitation.
- **Affluent + Contribution** → the legacy-driven wealthy buyer. Sell impact, family-office services, philanthropy.
- **Affluent + Growth** → the founder/intellectual wealthy buyer. Sell mastery, executive education, access.
- **Middle + Certainty** → the security-first professional. Sell insurance, savings, established brands.
- **Middle + Connection** → the family-first professional. Sell family experiences, vacations, kids' programs.
- **Middle + Growth** → the ambitious professional. Sell credentials, coaching, career development.
- **Working + Significance** → the status-seeking working-class buyer. Sell visible status (sneakers, phone, watch).
- **Working + Connection** → the family-driven working-class buyer. Sell anything that protects/elevates the family.
- **Working + Variety** → the escape-seeking working-class buyer. Sell entertainment, escape products. *Caution: this is where lottery and gambling extraction targets.*

## Puerto Rico application

PR's cultural defaults shift the needs landscape relative to mainland US norms:

- **Connection ranks higher than significance** across most tiers. Family, *familia, comunidad, parroquia* are deeply weighted. Selling to *significance* aggressively can backfire — it reads as *engreído*. Selling to *connection* usually wins.
- **Certainty is high post-María, post-Fiona, post-blackouts.** Trauma sensitizes need-priority. Many PR buyers across tiers weight certainty more than mainland equivalents. Lead with guarantees, track record, reliability.
- **Contribution shows up in faith vocabulary.** *"Bendiciones, servir, dar el ejemplo"* are contribution-need expressions. Listen for them; they reveal a buyer ready to be sold legacy products.
- **Significance can be served through family pride.** *"Esto pone bien alto el apellido"* (this raises the family name high) is a significance-frame that lands without triggering the *engreído* reflex.
- **Variety is real but often expressed through travel.** PR buyers love novelty in travel (Europe, Asia, US mainland trips) more than in luxury goods. Frame accordingly.
