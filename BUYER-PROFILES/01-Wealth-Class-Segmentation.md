# 01 — Wealth-Class Segmentation

*The most important buyer-profiling lens in this entire archive. Skip this and you'll spend a career selling the wrong thing to the wrong people.*

## The core insight

> **"Sell time to the rich. Sell aspiration to the middle class. Sell hope to the working class."**

This isn't cynicism — it's empirical observation across decades of consumer research, refined by every elite sales organization. Different income brackets have different problems, different fears, and different definitions of "value." A pitch optimized for one segment will land flat or insulting in another.

This playbook unpacks four wealth tiers, what each actually buys (vs. what they say they want), what they fear, how to sell to them, and — critically — what to *never* sell them.

---

## Tier 1 — The Affluent / Wealthy
*Liquid net worth $1M+, or household income $300K+, or significant business equity*

### What they have
Money. Optionality. Multiple homes or one premium home. Stable income or asset base. Access to professional services. Choices.

### What they actually lack
**Time.** Energy. Attention. Privacy. Discretion. Peace. Family harmony. Specialist expertise on niche topics. Trustworthy people. Authentic experiences.

### What they fear
- Loss of status (the hardest fear to articulate but the strongest driver)
- Tax exposure and audits
- Lawsuits and litigation
- Family discord over inheritance and succession
- Health decline and mortality
- Becoming irrelevant
- Being seen as gullible (they've been pitched a thousand times)

### What they actually buy

| Category | What "value" means to them |
|---|---|
| **Time-saving services** | Concierge, white-glove, full done-for-you, personal assistants, executive support |
| **Status & exclusivity** | Members-only, by-invitation, private banking, the suite, the box, the rare table |
| **Legacy & continuity** | Estate planning, family-office services, naming opportunities, foundations |
| **Expertise** | Specialists, second opinions, "the best in the world at X" |
| **Privacy & discretion** | NDAs, anonymized service, off-the-record handling |
| **Health, longevity, vitality** | Premium medicine, executive physicals, anti-aging, peak performance coaching |
| **Rare experiences** | Once-in-a-lifetime travel, curated cultural access, unique adventures |
| **Optionality** | Keeping doors open, hedges, second passports, geographic flexibility |
| **Trusted advisors** | The lawyer, the doctor, the wealth manager, the consigliere |

### How to sell to them

- **Lead with their time saved**, not features. "This gives you back 8 hours a week" beats "this has 47 features."
- **Premium price is a filter, not a barrier.** Cheap looks suspicious. A price that's *too low* will eliminate you from consideration.
- **Show track record with similar clientele** — anonymized, but specific. "I work with three family-office principals on the island."
- **Slow the pace.** They're not in a rush; if you appear rushed, you appear inexperienced.
- **Never appear needy.** The minute you need this deal, you've lost leverage. Klaff's frame control matters most here.
- **Refer them in their own circles.** Bring value (introductions, deals, opportunities) before extracting.
- **Use scarcity sparingly and only when real.** They're allergic to manufactured urgency — they invented it.
- **Respect their advisors.** Get the lawyer/accountant/CFO on your side; never bypass them.

### What to NEVER sell them

- Generic productivity hacks (they have an assistant for that)
- Lottery-mindset transformations (they're already "there")
- Hard scarcity tactics like fake countdowns (insulting)
- "Get rich quick" anything
- Anything that implies they need to "level up" their identity
- Cheap-looking proposals or pitch decks

### Puerto Rico application — the affluent tier

The PR affluent segment is concentrated and identifiable:

- **Established family-business *dueños*** — second/third-generation owners of significant private businesses (construction, distribution, food, automotive dealership groups, healthcare networks)
- **Top legal and accounting partners** — the senior partners of San Juan firms; old-money families; long professional reputations
- **Senior medical specialists** — surgeons, specialists in private practice, owners of medical groups
- **Act 60 / Act 22 transplants** — crypto, finance, founder-exit, hedge-fund principals who relocated post-2012
- **Real estate dynasties** — the families with named commercial buildings, marinas, hotels
- **Diaspora elite returners** — high-net-worth *boricuas* returning from Wall Street, Silicon Valley, Miami, NYC

Selling to this tier in PR specifically:

- **Trusted referral is the only real door.** Cold outreach to this segment is largely wasted effort.
- **The advisor network is small.** The same 50 lawyers, 30 accountants, 20 wealth managers. Get on their good side or stay outside.
- **Discretion is a virtue.** PR is small — gossip travels in days. If you can't keep a confidence, you're done in this tier.
- **Bilingual elegance matters.** Both languages, both cultures, no awkwardness.
- **Don’t look poor.** Quality watch, decent shoes, respectable car. They notice. (This isn't about wealth display — it's about competence signaling.)
- **The *finca* invitation is the signal.** When an affluent PR client invites you to the family *finca* or boat, you've crossed from vendor to trusted advisor. Treat the access accordingly.

## Tier 2 — The Middle Class / Aspirational Professional
*Household income $75K–$250K, stable jobs or growing small businesses, mortgaged homes, college-educated*

### What they have
Stable income (probably). Some savings. A 401(k) or local equivalent. Mortgage. Car payments. Kids in school. Vacation budget. Real ambition. Career trajectory.

### What they actually lack
- A financial cushion deep enough to weather a real shock
- Time for self-development and family simultaneously
- Clarity on what their next 10 years should look like
- Confidence that they're "making it"
- A definitive path to the next tier

### What they fear
- Job loss and being replaced
- Their kids falling behind academically or socially
- Medical emergencies destroying savings
- Falling behind peers (visible social comparison drives this hard)
- Aging without enough retirement savings
- Their professional skills becoming obsolete (especially in tech, finance, marketing)

### What they actually buy

| Category | What "value" means to them |
|---|---|
| **Aspiration ladders** | The next-better neighborhood, next-better school, next-better car, next-better title |
| **Security products** | Insurance, savings vehicles, college funds, retirement plans |
| **Children's advantages** | Tutoring, sports clubs, music lessons, college prep, summer programs, private school |
| **Education and credentials** | Master's degrees, MBAs, certifications, professional coaching |
| **Convenience that frees evenings** | Meal kits, mid-tier housekeeping, dog walking, lawn service |
| **Status proxies** | Premium-but-attainable brands (Lexus, Apple, BMW 3-series, Whole Foods, Nespresso) |
| **Travel** | The annual family trip, the cruise, the Disney pilgrimage, the European tour |
| **Wellness and self-improvement** | Gym memberships, therapy, productivity courses, fitness coaching |
| **Home upgrades** | Renovations, smart home, solar panels (especially in PR) |

### How to sell to them

- **Aspirational positioning + concrete ROI math.** Show where this puts them next year, in five years, when their kids are in college.
- **Monthly payment framing.** They budget monthly. "$1,400/month" beats "$67,000 total" even when math is identical.
- **Reference families like theirs achieving the next step.** Specifics matter — same income bracket, same neighborhood, same kid-stage.
- **Before-and-after framing.** "Where you are now → where you'll be." Pink's Pixar pitch works beautifully here.
- **Address the spouse explicitly.** Middle-class consequential decisions are usually joint. If you sell to one spouse only, expect a 50% no-show on close day.
- **Honor the kids angle.** Anything that helps the children land harder than anything that helps the parent personally.
- **Provide professional-grade proposals.** This segment is educated, judges presentation, and will Google-research everything you say.

### What to NEVER sell them

- Pure status with no functional benefit (they'll skip; they have a budget)
- Pure transformation with no concrete plan (they're skeptical of "magic")
- Anything that strains the family budget without clear ROI
- High-pressure scarcity tactics (they distrust hype — most have been burned)
- "Get rich quick" anything — they've already chosen the slow-and-steady path

### Puerto Rico application — the middle class

The PR middle class is the **biggest addressable market** for most products and services on the island:

- **Government employees and managers** — stable, pensionable, predictable income
- **Bank and financial-services professionals** — Banco Popular, Oriental, FirstBank middle and senior staff
- **Healthcare professionals** — nurses, mid-career physicians, pharmacy owners, dental practices
- **Engineers, IT, tech** — utility companies, telecoms, growing software firms, telework professionals
- **Educators** — university professors, private-school teachers, school administrators
- **Small-business owners** — restaurant owners, retail shop owners, service-business owners (the engine of metro PR)
- **Lawyers and accountants** — mid-career, not yet partner, not yet wealthy

Selling to this tier in PR specifically:

- **The spouse is in the conversation, period.** *"Lo voy a consultar con mi esposa/esposo"* is real. Sell to both or accept the 50% drop.
- **Schools and kids drive aspirational purchases.** Insurance, real estate, financial products, even cars — frame around the kids' future and conversion climbs dramatically.
- **Monthly payment culture.** *Financiamiento* matters. "En 36 mensualidades" sells what "$X total" can't.
- **The diaspora comparison is constant.** Many in this tier have siblings or cousins on the mainland. *"Mi prima en Orlando paga la mitad"* is a real objection — prepare for it.
- **Spanish-language pride matters here.** This tier is bilingual but identifies strongly as *boricua*; don't talk down their language preference.
- **The vacation budget is real.** Annual trip to Orlando, the cruise, the Dominican Republic getaway — these are sacred. Don't suggest cutting them; redirect *around* them.

## Tier 3 — The Working Class / Aspirational
*Household income $25K–$75K, hourly work or small-business operators, often paycheck-to-paycheck, deep family obligations*

### What they have
Limited disposable income. Their family. Their faith. Their community. Strong work ethic. Big dreams (often unspoken). Resilience built from circumstance.

### What they actually lack
- Financial cushion — one emergency from crisis
- Formal-education access (often capped at high school or some college)
- Time and energy — multiple jobs are common
- Professional networks that open doors
- Access to good information about money, health, opportunity

### What they fear
- Medical emergencies (the #1 financial ruin in this tier)
- Job loss or hours cut
- Eviction or foreclosure
- Their kids ending up no better off than they are
- Falling further behind
- Being looked down on or disrespected

### What they actually buy

| Category | What "value" means to them |
|---|---|
| **Hope and transformation** | "This could change my life" — books, programs, side-hustles, MLM, get-rich-quick schemes |
| **Lottery-mindset products** | Actual lottery, scratch tickets, sports betting, casino, day-trading apps |
| **Religion and community** | Church tithing, *parranda*, *velorios*, gospel events, faith conferences |
| **Status signals** | Premium sneakers, latest phone, gold chains, car rims, manicures, fresh haircut |
| **Their children's better future** | School uniforms, electronics for school, quinceañeras, birthday parties (often at financial cost) |
| **Entertainment & escape** | Music, sports, gaming, alcohol, weed, streaming, lottery itself |
| **Food** | Quality food matters culturally and emotionally — not negotiable even on tight budget |
| **Tools of the trade** | If they can earn more with a better tool (truck, equipment, license), they buy |

### How to sell to them

- **Sell dreams responsibly.** Concrete next steps, not just "transformation." If you can't deliver the promise, refuse the sale.
- **Easy payment terms.** Weekly, biweekly, *fiao*, layaway, financing with low monthlies. Match their cash flow rhythm.
- **Community proof outranks expert proof.** "My neighbor Pedro got the same training and now he makes $X" beats academic credentials.
- **Cultural-insider language.** Corporate-speak alienates; *boricua* register lands. *"Pa' que tú veas"* > *"As you can observe."*
- **WhatsApp, in-person, neighborhood channels.** Email and LinkedIn miss this tier. Community events, parish bulletins, *Facebook Marketplace*, local radio, TV ads, *barrio*-level word of mouth.
- **Lead with what they get *fast*.** This tier discounts the future heavily — not because they're irrational, but because the future has betrayed them before. Show the first benefit in week one.
- **Respect, always.** This tier has been condescended to their whole lives. The seller who treats them with dignity wins.

### What to NEVER sell them

- Generic "premium" branding (irrelevant; they care about function or community status, not lifestyle status)
- Anything that requires them to "already have arrived"
- Long-term-only payoffs with no immediate visible benefit
- Multi-year subscriptions with high upfront costs
- **Predatory products of any kind** — see ethics section below

### ⚠️ Ethical alert — the responsibility line

This tier is the most vulnerable to exploitation because the same psychology that makes "sell hope" effective is what makes the following predatory:

- High-interest payday loans and *prestamistas*
- MLM and pyramid schemes targeted at people who can't afford the inventory
- "Get rich quick" courses sold to people whose savings would be wiped by the purchase
- Insurance products that pay out only in scenarios that never apply
- Title loans on the family car
- Crypto or trading platforms gamified to extract from inexperienced users
- Religion-adjacent "prosperity gospel" extraction
- Lottery and gambling marketed as financial strategy

**A seller with integrity refuses to deploy the techniques in this section against people who cannot afford the product, who lack the context to evaluate it, or whose interests would be harmed by the purchase.** This is the line. Cross it and the long-term cost — reputational, karmic, legal — always exceeds the short-term gain.

### Puerto Rico application — the working class

This is **a significant share of the island's population**, concentrated in:

- Service workers (hospitality, retail, food service)
- Construction and trades (electricians, plumbers, masons — some of whom are doing well; others are paycheck-to-paycheck)
- Agriculture workers (coffee, citrus, plantain)
- Small-shop owners in non-metro areas (*colmados*, beauty salons, auto repair, *kioskos*)
- Domestic workers and home health aides
- Post-María displaced households still recovering
- Many rural municipalities (Utuado, Las Marías, Maricao, Vieques, Adjuntas, Aguas Buenas, etc.)

Selling to this tier in PR specifically:

- **The local *colmado* and *barbería* are real channels.** Distribution and word-of-mouth happen there.
- **Holiday and event-tied purchases.** *Reyes, Navidades, Acción de Gracias, Madres, Padres, quinceañeras, graduaciones* — these moments unlock spending that doesn't otherwise happen.
- **"Pay weekly" is a real product feature**, not a discount tactic. Match the rhythm.
- **Cash culture is alive.** Many in this tier still operate substantially in cash. Plan for it.
- **Family-network purchases.** One person buys, the whole family considers buying. Lean into family demos and group rates.
- **Government programs intersect.** *PAN, WIC, Mi Salud, Section 8* — know which apply, refer ethically when they help, never sell substitutes for services people qualify for free.
- **Reggaeton and traditional radio still convert.** Don't dismiss "old" media — it has more reach here than mainland averages would suggest.

## Tier 4 — Subsistence / Survival
*Household income under $25K, or precarious housing, food, or medical access*

### What they have
Resourcefulness. Strong family bonds (often). Religion. Survival skills. Sometimes — government assistance.

### What they actually lack
Everything that the higher tiers take for granted. Stable housing. Stable food. Reliable transportation. Medical access. Time. Cushion. Hope, sometimes.

### What they fear
Everything more acutely than the working-class tier. Plus: dependence, shame, losing children to the system, eviction, hunger, untreated illness.

### What they actually buy

Minimal. Necessities. Day-to-day survival. Cheap immediate comfort. Lottery (the dream cost). Often whatever credit they can access.

### ⚠️ Ethical hard line

**A seller with integrity does not sell aspirational, luxury, or high-margin products to the subsistence tier.** The right relationship with this segment is:

- Refer to government programs, NGOs, community resources, *iglesia* networks
- Provide free or cooperative pricing where the product genuinely improves their situation
- Offer financial literacy without selling product
- Connect them to upward-mobility resources (free training, JobsCorp, community college, *Centros Sor Isolina Ferré*, etc.)

If your business model depends on selling to this tier at full price, **reconsider the business model.** The PR market is rich in legitimate opportunity higher up the wealth distribution; predation downward isn't necessary.

### Where this tier needs to be served

- Cooperatives, mutual aid, *cooperativas* (a strong PR tradition)
- NGOs and faith-based assistance
- Government program enrollment assistance (legitimately, not as a fee-extracting service)
- Free or near-free education and skill training
- Health-fair and clinic outreach
- Disaster response and recovery

## The summary table

| Tier | Sell them… | Lead with… | Avoid… |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Affluent** | Time, status, legacy, expertise, privacy, optionality | Track record, discretion, time-saved math | Generic productivity, hype, fake scarcity |
| **Middle class** | Aspiration, security, kids' future, education, convenience | Concrete ROI, monthly math, family framing | Status-only, magic transformation, budget-strain |
| **Working class** | Hope, transformation, community, kids' future, status signals | Fast first benefit, easy payments, neighbor proof | Premium branding, long payoffs, predatory anything |
| **Subsistence** | (Don't sell aspirational; serve, refer, educate) | Free resources, dignity, basic-need solutions | Anything high-margin or aspirational |

## How to identify the tier in 90 seconds

Before the pitch, observe and ask:

- **Their car** (or how they arrived)
- **Their dress and watch**
- **Their references** (neighborhoods, schools, vacations, brands)
- **Their questions** (Affluent: "What's the timeline?" Middle: "What's the monthly?" Working: "How fast can I see results?")
- **Their language register** (Spanish/English mix, formality level, vocabulary range)
- **Their stated occupation and seniority**
- **The decision pattern they describe** (solo? spouse? family? advisor team?)

Don't ask them to self-classify. Read the signals. Adjust the pitch.
