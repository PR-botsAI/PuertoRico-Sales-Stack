# 06 — Puerto Rico Buyer Archetypes

*The local-specific personas that don't show up in mainland sales literature but show up constantly in PR sales reality. Read these carefully — they'll save you years of trial-and-error.*

## Why this lens matters

You can know wealth class, generation, DISC, and B2B role — and still miss what makes PR sales unique. The island has buyer archetypes that don't translate cleanly from mainland frameworks. These archetypes carry implicit cultural rules, communication codes, decision patterns, and trust signals that aren't documented in Cialdini or Voss.

This playbook profiles eleven archetypes you'll encounter. Each profile includes how to recognize them, how to sell to them, and what to never do.

---

## 1. *El Dueño* — The Family-Business Owner

**Who:** First-, second-, or third-generation owner of a privately held PR business. Construction, distribution, retail, food, automotive, professional services, healthcare. The single most common high-value buyer archetype on the island.

**How to recognize:**
- Office in or attached to the business itself, not a corporate tower
- Photos of the family business through the decades on the walls
- First-name basis with everyone in the operation
- Decision-making is personal; reports to no board
- Often physically present in the business operation despite owning it
- May have a humble car; status displayed differently

**What they care about:**
- Family legacy and what happens after them
- Employees they've had for 20+ years (genuine loyalty)
- The business reputation in their community
- Their *palabra* (their word) — their personal honor in deals
- Cash flow and operational stability
- Protecting the business from outside disruption

**How to sell to *El Dueño*:**
- **Build the personal relationship before the business one.** Multiple meetings, family inquiry, *cariño*. The transactional approach loses every time.
- **Honor their legacy.** Ask about the founder (often a parent or grandparent), the business's history, its place in the community.
- **Sell to longevity.** Frame around how this strengthens the business for the next generation.
- **Be the bilingual professional.** Switch languages as they do; never make them work harder linguistically.
- **Respect their employees.** A seller who treats the receptionist condescendingly is dead in this deal.
- **Verbal commitment matters.** A *dueño*'s yes is binding; treat it that way.
- **Be present in their world.** Drop by occasionally; attend industry events they attend; show up at community moments.

**What NEVER to do:**
- Skip the relational opening
- Imply they're "behind the times"
- Pressure or manufacture urgency
- Try to bypass them in the org
- Be late, disorganized, or sloppy in proposals
- Promise what you can't deliver — their network will know within a week

## 2. *El Profesional* — The Mid-Career Professional

**Who:** Lawyer, accountant, doctor, engineer, banker, manager, government professional. Mid-30s to mid-50s. College-educated, often with mainland exposure. Stable income, professional ambitions, family obligations.

**How to recognize:**
- Office or workspace in a corporate or professional building
- Bilingual fluency, switches naturally between Spanish and English
- Cars: Toyota, Honda, Lexus, BMW 3-series, Mercedes C-class — nothing flashy
- Family photos on desk; kids' photos prominent
- Calendar is full; replies efficiently to email
- Wears business casual; degrees framed on the wall

**What they care about:**
- Career trajectory and the next step
- Children's education and future
- Time efficiency (they're overcommitted)
- Professional reputation
- Financial security and retirement planning
- Spouse's opinion on major decisions

**How to sell to *El Profesional*:**
- Lead with substance and respect their intelligence
- Bilingual proposals; clean digital presence
- Show ROI in concrete numbers
- Reference similar professionals as case studies
- Be email-responsive; respect their calendar
- Address the spouse openly; offer joint meetings on family decisions
- Frame around kids' future when applicable

**What NEVER to do:**
- Talk down to them (they have credentials and they notice)
- Be sloppy with data — they'll catch errors
- Push for same-day decisions on consequential matters
- Skip the spouse on family-impacting deals

## 3. *La Doña / El Don* — The Senior Decision-Maker

**Who:** 60+ buyer, often retired professional, business owner emeritus, or matriarch/patriarch of an extended family. May be the parent-funder of younger family decisions. Holds informal but real power in family-business and household decisions.

**How to recognize:**
- *Don/Doña* + first name is the natural address
- Lifetime in the community; everyone knows them
- Spanish-language preference, often
- Phone-first communication; WhatsApp adopted but less native
- Decisions made deliberately, often after family discussion

**What they care about:**
- Family welfare and reputation
- Their legacy and what they leave behind
- Health (their own and their spouse's)
- The community they helped build
- The next generation doing right
- Faith and tradition

**How to sell to *La Doña / El Don*:**
- Maximum respect register: *don/doña*, *usted*, formal Spanish
- Phone or in-person preferred over digital
- Slow pace; multiple touchpoints across weeks
- Acknowledge their experience explicitly
- Reference adult children when relevant (they often fund or influence decisions)
- Be patient with stories about the past — they're earning your respect
- Show up for community moments (funerals, weddings, *quinceañeras*) when invited

**What NEVER to do:**
- Use first name without invitation
- Skip courtesies (greeting family, asking after health)
- Hurry the process
- Use English-default communication
- Be informally dressed in their presence

## 4. *El/La Diaspora Returnee*

**Who:** A *boricua* who lived on the mainland (often NYC, Florida, Boston, Texas) for 5–30 years and has returned to PR. Brings mainland habits, sometimes with mainland income, often with mainland-formed expectations.

**How to recognize:**
- Code-switches with ease; sometimes English-default in business
- References mainland brands, restaurants, services casually
- Has "home" in two places mentally
- May be older than the average buyer in their wealth tier
- Often nostalgic and idealistic about PR culture, with sharper critique than mainlanders allow themselves

**What they care about:**
- Reconnecting with PR identity and family
- Bringing mainland standards of service/quality to PR life
- Sometimes: distance from mainland stresses (commute, weather, politics)
- Often: contributing to the island's growth
- Family (often elderly parents who drew them back)

**How to sell to the Diaspora Returnee:**
- Bilingual fluency, both ways
- Mainland-standard professionalism + PR-warmth (both required)
- Reference both worlds in case studies and examples
- Acknowledge the bridging identity — don't ask them to be just *boricua* or just American
- Be straight about what works here vs. what doesn't (they appreciate frankness)

**What NEVER to do:**
- Assume they're either fully *boricua* or fully mainland
- Be defensive about PR's challenges — they know them
- Push them into one cultural frame

## 5. *El Act-60 Transplant*

**Who:** US (or international) high-net-worth individual who relocated to PR primarily for tax incentives under Act 22 / Act 60. Often crypto, finance, founder-exit. Concentrated in Dorado, Condado, Old San Juan, Palmas del Mar, Guaynabo. Generally limited Spanish.

**How to recognize:**
- English-default communication
- Mainland or international references; limited local network
- Concentrated in specific neighborhoods
- High disposable income, often higher than displayed
- May have a *bona fide* tax setup (full residency) or be in transition

**What they care about:**
- Maintaining Act 60 compliance (this is non-negotiable for many)
- Finding trusted local professionals (CPA, attorney, real estate, doctors)
- Quality of life (security, schools, amenities)
- Social network in PR (often slow to develop)
- Investment opportunities they understand

**How to sell to the Act 60 Transplant:**
- English-default; bilingual is a bonus, not required
- Mainland-level professionalism in all materials
- Understand their tax structure if you're selling anything financial
- Provide warmth they may not be getting elsewhere on the island
- Refer them to other trusted local professionals (you become more valuable)
- Be patient with their adjustment to PR pace

**What NEVER to do:**
- Resent or moralize about their tax status (lose them in one comment)
- Treat them as outsiders explicitly (they're sensitive about it)
- Recommend Spanish-language-only services
- Pretend you understand Act 60 if you don't — they have detailed knowledge

## 6. *La Abuela* — The Grandmother / Family Matriarch

**Who:** Older woman, often grandmother, often the household's central trust figure. May not be the *named* buyer but is frequently the actual decision-influencer for family-impacting purchases. Funds many family decisions through gifts or *prestamitos*.

**How to recognize:**
- Present at family decisions formally or informally
- Often referred to even when not in the room ("voy a hablar con abuela")
- High trust within the family network
- Spanish-default; sometimes limited English
- Strong faith / religious anchoring

**What they care about:**
- Grandchildren above all
- Family unity and harmony
- Health (her own and the family's)
- Modesty and avoiding *fanfarronería*
- Faith and tradition
- The family's reputation

**How to sell when *La Abuela* is involved:**
- Recognize her presence and address her with maximum respect
- Frame around grandchildren's welfare when possible
- Use *usted* and *doña* + first name
- Don't push fast; her endorsement comes slowly but is decisive
- Provide simple, dignified materials — Spanish, large print if relevant, no jargon
- Honor her role as advisor even when she's not the formal buyer

**What NEVER to do:**
- Dismiss her as not the buyer
- Use English-default in her presence
- Pressure her or the family in her presence
- Sell her things she doesn't need

## 7. *El Resolvedor*

**Who:** The person in any organization or family who *resuelve* — makes problems go away through networks, ingenuity, and willingness to figure it out. Not always senior in title; usually disproportionately influential. Can be the office manager, the operations lead, the family fixer, the small-business right hand.

**How to recognize:**
- Phone rings constantly; people come to them with problems
- Has a network across departments, vendors, agencies
- Knows everyone by first name
- Solves things that don't seem solvable through formal channels
- Doesn't take credit publicly; takes credit privately

**What they care about:**
- Being seen as competent and indispensable
- Solving the problem
- Maintaining their network of contacts
- Pride in their *resolver* identity
- Respect from peers and superiors

**How to sell to *El Resolvedor*:**
- Make their job easier; reduce their friction
- Acknowledge their network and competence
- Provide tools that make them more *resolver*, not less
- Be a *resolver* yourself in their eyes
- Don't bypass them — you'll lose internal access permanently
- Send referrals their way; ask them for referrals

**What NEVER to do:**
- Treat them as a low-level functionary
- Make their job harder with complex processes
- Try to go over their head on operational matters

## 8. *El Cangrejo* — The Skeptic / Negative-Anchor

**Who:** The buyer (or family member, or coworker) who pulls others down toward skepticism, distrust, or status quo. Named for the *cangrejo* (crab) that pulls others back into the bucket. Every PR sales process encounters at least one. Often well-intentioned, often legitimately burned by past sellers, sometimes politically motivated.

**How to recognize:**
- Skeptical questions disproportionate to the stake
- Stories about being burned before
- "Aquí eso no funciona" / "Eso aquí no se puede" as recurring phrases
- Pulls the conversation negative when others are positive
- Often has history with a competitor or past failure

**What they care about:**
- Protecting against being burned again
- Not letting others be naïve
- Sometimes: legitimate concerns that are real
- Sometimes: jealousy or political reasons unrelated to your offer

**How to handle *El Cangrejo*:**
- Acknowledge their concerns explicitly — don't dismiss
- Ask what specifically went wrong before; address it concretely
- Don't try to convert pure cangrejos; neutralize them
- Build the deal around your champion and decision-maker; treat the cangrejo's objections seriously but don't let them dominate
- Offer pilot or low-commitment first step to reduce their risk frame
- Recognize when the cangrejo has legitimate insight — sometimes they're right

**What NEVER to do:**
- Dismiss their concerns publicly
- Pretend they're not in the room
- Mock or contradict them in front of others
- Try to win them over through pure charm or pressure

## 9. *El/La Hustler* — The Aspirational Entrepreneur

**Who:** Young (20s–30s), often working multiple income streams, building a brand or business, deeply online, often metro-area. Aspirational consumer of business education, courses, software, services. The cohort Hormozi's content speaks most directly to.

**How to recognize:**
- Multiple income mentions: "freelance side," "my brand," "the agency"
- Active on Instagram, TikTok, sometimes YouTube
- Knows current creator-economy references
- Consumes business content (podcasts, YouTube long-form)
- Bilingual but often English-leading in business contexts
- Style is contemporary, brand-conscious

**What they care about:**
- Scaling income and freedom
- Building a personal brand
- Learning and applying fast
- Networking with other ambitious peers
- Proof of trajectory (theirs and yours)

**How to sell to the *Hustler*:**
- Modern brand and marketing; if your assets look dated, you lose them
- Active social presence required
- Use modern frameworks (Value Equation, Core Four, Dream 100) — they know the language
- Show specific outcomes for similar-profile clients
- Provide community access; this cohort values peer network
- Easy digital onboarding; mobile-first
- Payment plans for higher-ticket programs

**What NEVER to do:**
- Look old, corporate, or out of touch
- Use Boomer-style sales tactics (long phone calls, paper proposals)
- Be condescending about their age or experience
- Sell them generic content they can find on YouTube for free

## 10. *El Trabajador Honesto* — The Honest Worker

**Who:** Working-class household, steady job (factory, hospitality, healthcare aide, trades, retail, food service, agricultural). Hard-working, family-anchored, often religious. The backbone of the island's labor force and a significant consumer segment.

**How to recognize:**
- Direct, unpretentious communication
- Spanish-default, sometimes only Spanish
- Modest dress and demeanor
- Strong family identity
- May reference *iglesia* and faith naturally
- Cash and *fiao* culture
- Weekly or biweekly pay rhythm

**What they care about:**
- Family welfare
- Honest dealing
- Faith and community
- Children's future
- Not being taken advantage of
- Their *palabra* (their word) and yours

**How to sell to *El Trabajador Honesto*:**
- Respect as the baseline
- Spanish-native communication
- Honesty over polish — they read polish as suspect
- Weekly/biweekly payment plans where possible
- Community references (their neighbors, their *barrio*, their parish)
- Patience — they consult family and trusted advisors
- WhatsApp, in-person, *colmado* networks
- Faith-respectful framing when relevant

**What NEVER to do:**
- Talk down or simplify in a condescending way
- Use English-default
- Pressure for fast decisions
- Sell predatorily — they're a vulnerable segment despite their dignity
- Be slick or showy

---

## 11. *El Buscavidas / La Buscavidas*

**Who:** The hustling spirit — often working-class, sometimes middle-class, who is *constantly* looking for the next opportunity. May juggle a *chiripa* (side hustle), a small business, a service offering, and a day job all at once. Resourceful, network-oriented, optimistic, sometimes overcommitted.

**How to recognize:**
- Multiple cards or multiple roles
- WhatsApp constantly active
- Refers people constantly (and expects referrals back)
- Big plans, varying execution capability
- Spanish-default; relational style
- Often the connector in their community

**What they care about:**
- Income generation
- Opportunities for themselves and their network
- Being respected as enterprising, not as poor
- Their reputation as someone who delivers
- Their family (always)

**How to sell to the *Buscavidas*:**
- Honor their hustle; never make them feel small for it
- Show how your offering helps them earn more or save time
- Make them a partner or referral source where appropriate
- WhatsApp-first communication
- Easy payment terms
- Quick wins; concrete first benefits
- Respect their time — they're juggling

**What NEVER to do:**
- Dismiss their multiple ventures as scattered
- Sell them complex multi-month programs without quick wins
- Be slow to respond on WhatsApp
- Refer them only as buyers and never as partners

## The combined-archetype matrix

Many buyers are blends. Common PR blends:

- **Dueño + Don** — the older family-business patriarch (very common; trust-building is multi-month)
- **Profesional + Diaspora Returnee** — the bilingual returning expert (common in Act 60 advisory and corporate sales)
- **Hustler + Buscavidas** — the younger online entrepreneur with multiple gigs (huge cohort, Spanish-content opportunity)
- **Abuela + Trabajador Honesto** — the working-class matriarch (highly protective of the family)
- **Profesional + Resolvedor** — the mid-level operations expert in mid-market companies

Profile the archetype, then layer wealth class, generation, DISC, and B2B role for full picture.
