# 05 — B2B Decision Roles

*Every B2B deal has seven roles in the room — most of them invisible if you're not looking. Ignore one and the deal stalls. Identify and sell to each appropriately and the deal closes 3x faster.*

## Why this lens matters

Most sellers focus on the *primary contact* — the person who answered the phone, attended the demo, asked for the proposal. That person is rarely the sole decision-maker. They might not even be the most important person in the deal. The Strategic Selling literature (Miller & Heiman) and the Challenger research (Dixon & Adamson) both confirm what veteran sellers know: **complex B2B sales are won by mapping the full buying committee and serving each role appropriately.**

This playbook breaks down the seven roles, what each one cares about, how to identify them, and how to sell to each — with explicit PR family-business calibration.

## The seven roles

### 1. The Economic Buyer
*"The one who can sign the check."*

This is the person with the authority to release the funds. In small businesses, often the owner. In mid-market, often the CFO or department head. In enterprise, often a VP or higher.

**What they care about:**
- ROI and financial return
- Risk to the business
- Strategic fit with broader goals
- Their own credibility (this purchase will be associated with them)

**How to identify them:**
- They're the person referenced when others say "I'll need to check with…"
- They control the budget line, not just the budget request
- Their meeting time is harder to get
- Their email signature includes financial or executive title

**How to sell to the Economic Buyer:**
- Lead with business outcomes, not features
- Speak in their financial language (ROI, payback period, NPV if relevant)
- Acknowledge risk explicitly and de-risk concretely
- Keep meetings short and high-density (their time is the constraint)
- Get a champion to bring you in; rarely walk in cold

**PR-specific:** In family businesses, the Economic Buyer is almost always the *dueño* personally — even on "small" decisions that mainland equivalents would delegate. Don't assume the operations manager has signing authority. Confirm.

### 2. The Technical Buyer
*"The one who can say no."*

This person can't approve the deal, but they can veto it. They evaluate against technical, regulatory, or compliance standards. In SaaS: often IT or security. In manufacturing: often engineering or operations. In regulated industries: often legal or compliance.

**What they care about:**
- Will this break what we have?
- Does it meet our standards/specs/compliance?
- What's the technical risk?
- Is the vendor competent in detail?

**How to identify them:**
- They ask the most specific, technical questions
- They request documentation: SOC 2, ISO, security questionnaires, technical specs
- They have veto language: "I can't approve this unless…"

**How to sell to the Technical Buyer:**
- Get them the documentation they need fast — don't make them ask twice
- Treat their concerns as legitimate, not as obstacles
- Provide a technical contact on your side (engineer, architect, security lead)
- Acknowledge what your solution does *not* do — honesty wins this audience
- Address their specific compliance/regulatory framework by name

**PR-specific:** In regulated industries on the island (banking, healthcare, insurance, government), the Technical Buyer often includes both US federal compliance *and* PR-specific regulatory layers. Know both. The seller who only handles mainland compliance loses to one who handles both.

### 3. The User Buyer
*"The one who will use it daily."*

These are the people whose work changes if the deal closes. Often the people who initiated the search — they have the problem. But they rarely have buying authority.

**What they care about:**
- Will this make my job easier or harder?
- Will it make me look good or bad?
- Is there a learning curve?
- Do I trust the vendor to support me?

**How to identify them:**
- They use the product language: "When we use X tool, we struggle with Y"
- They have specific pain points and workflow knowledge
- They're enthusiastic in demos when others are neutral
- They reference "my team" or "my workflow"

**How to sell to the User Buyer:**
- Demo *to their actual workflow*, not a generic demo
- Show specific time-saved or pain-reduced for their job
- Promise (and deliver) onboarding and support
- Make them look good by referencing them in front of leadership
- Give them adoption tools, training, documentation
- *They are the most likely Champion.* Treat them well; they sell internally.

**PR-specific:** PR User Buyers often have outsized internal influence because tenure is longer than mainland averages — someone who's been with the company 20 years carries more weight even at a non-executive title than the org chart suggests.

### 4. The Champion
*"The one who sells the deal internally when you're not in the room."*

The Champion is not a role on the org chart — it's someone (often a User Buyer, sometimes a Technical Buyer, occasionally an Economic Buyer themselves) who has decided your solution should win and is willing to advocate for it internally. **No complex B2B deal closes without one.**

**What they care about:**
- The same things their role normally cares about
- *Plus*: the deal succeeding, because their reputation is now linked
- *Plus*: looking good for having found the solution

**How to identify them:**
- They volunteer information you didn't ask for
- They introduce you to other stakeholders proactively
- They share internal context ("here's how our CFO thinks about this")
- They take meetings without being chased
- They use "we" language about your solution before you've sold it

**How to support a Champion:**
- Arm them with internal-selling materials: business cases, ROI calculators, executive summaries
- Coach them on objections they'll face from others
- Give them early access, beta features, executive briefings — anything that elevates them internally
- Don't burn them: never go around them, never let them be surprised by something you said to another stakeholder
- Send them references and case studies they can forward
- Recognize them by name in mutual contacts

**PR-specific:** PR Champions often have personal relationships across the organization that go beyond the org chart — they went to school with the CFO, their families know each other, they share *padrinos*. Lean into these informal channels via your Champion.

### 5. The Coach
*"The one who tells you what's actually happening."*

Distinct from the Champion. A Coach may not advocate for you internally — they may not even prefer your solution — but they give you accurate information about the buying process, the politics, the competitors, the budget realities. The Coach is your reporter.

**What they care about:**
- Often something specific to their own goals (visibility, career advancement, helping you because you helped them)
- The process working transparently
- The right answer (whatever that is) being chosen

**How to identify them:**
- They share information that isn't formally theirs to share ("between you and me, the real concern is…")
- They volunteer competitive intelligence
- They tell you what's *not* working in your pitch
- They warn you about internal politics

**How to develop a Coach:**
- Give before you take (insight, introductions, referrals)
- Honor confidentiality absolutely — never use Coach intel in a way that betrays them
- Stay in touch even on deals you lose; Coaches are multi-deal assets
- Treat them with respect equal to or above the formal decision-makers

**PR-specific:** PR Coaches are gold because the island's tight network means a Coach in one organization often has visibility into three others. A great Coach can give you intel on the entire competitive landscape in a vertical. Cultivate two or three Coaches per industry.

### 6. The Gatekeeper
*"The one who controls access to the people you need."*

Executive assistants, office managers, schedulers. They decide whether your call gets through, whether your email gets forwarded, whether your meeting gets booked.

**What they care about:**
- Protecting their executive's time
- Being treated as professionals (which they are)
- Not being manipulated or talked down to

**How to identify them:**
- They're the named contact in inbound replies
- They have email addresses like "office of…" or "assistant to…"
- They're the ones answering when you call cold

**How to sell to the Gatekeeper:**
- Treat them with the same respect you'd give the executive — they remember and they tell
- Use their name; remember it
- Be honest about who you are and what you want
- Bring them value where you can (industry insight, helpful context)
- Make their job easier: confirm meetings, send agendas in advance, never be late
- *Never try to bypass them.* They will find out, and they will close every future door.

**PR-specific:** The Gatekeeper in a PR family business is often a long-tenured family friend or the *dueño*'s personal assistant of 20+ years. Their loyalty is fierce. Win them with consistent respect over months, not with charm in one call.

### 7. The Blocker
*"The one who opposes the deal."*

Every non-trivial B2B deal has at least one Blocker — someone who would prefer this not happen. Sometimes for legitimate reasons (incumbent vendor; loyalty; legitimate technical concern). Sometimes for political reasons (their pet project gets de-funded; their team loses scope). Sometimes for personal reasons (they have a relationship with a competitor).

**What they care about:**
- Defending the status quo, their turf, their preferred vendor, their existing investment
- Reasons — often legitimate, often unstated

**How to identify them:**
- Their feedback is consistently negative or skeptical
- They raise objections others don't share
- They were not the ones who initiated the search
- They have an investment in an incumbent solution

**How to handle a Blocker:**
- **Don't try to convert them.** It's expensive and often impossible.
- Instead, *neutralize* them — understand their concern, address what's addressable, accept what isn't
- Strengthen your Champion and Economic Buyer so the Blocker is outvoted
- Sometimes the right move is to *escalate above the Blocker* — if they're a mid-level objector and the Economic Buyer is committed
- Recognize that some deals are unwinnable because the Blocker has true power; don't waste cycle on them

**PR-specific:** Family-business Blockers can be family members not formally in operations — a sibling, an in-law, a cousin who feels their interest isn't being served. These are nearly impossible to convert and often kill deals quietly. The only defense is a strong relationship with the *dueño* who can override family politics.

## The mapping exercise

Before your next consequential B2B meeting, draw this grid:

| Role | Name | Their priority | Stage of buy-in | My next action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Economic Buyer | | | (Engaged / Lukewarm / Cold) | |
| Technical Buyer | | | | |
| User Buyer(s) | | | | |
| Champion | | | | |
| Coach | | | | |
| Gatekeeper | | | | |
| Blocker(s) | | | | |

If any row is blank — specifically Economic Buyer, Champion, or Blocker — that's your highest-priority gap to close. Deals don't close with gaps in these three rows.

## The PR family-business overlay

In PR family-business deals, **the roles often consolidate into one or two people** in ways mainland sellers find surprising:

- The *dueño* is often the Economic Buyer, Technical Buyer, *and* the Blocker on different dimensions
- The *dueño*'s spouse may be the invisible decision-maker even on "business" deals
- The longest-tenured employee (often a generation of the family) is often the Gatekeeper and Champion
- The *dueño*'s children working in the business may be User Buyers with veto power for the future

The practical move: in PR family business, map the **family** as carefully as you map the org chart. Many deals are decided around the dinner table, not the conference table.
