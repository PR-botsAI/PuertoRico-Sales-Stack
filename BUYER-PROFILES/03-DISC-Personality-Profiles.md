# 03 — DISC Personality Profiles

*The four communication styles. Read them from the buyer's first 90 seconds; calibrate everything that follows.*

## Why DISC matters in sales

DISC (Dominance / Influence / Steadiness / Conscientiousness) is the most operationally useful personality framework for sellers. Unlike MBTI (16 types; complex; debated psychometric validity), DISC has four practical types you can spot in real time and adjust to within minutes. It's been used in corporate sales training since the 1970s for one reason: it works.

The core insight: **the same pitch lands differently with different personality types.** A pitch that nails a Driver bores a Steady. A pitch that warms an Influencer alarms a Conscientious. Calibrate or lose deals you should have won.

## The four types

### D — Dominance / Driver
*Fast, decisive, direct, results-focused, competitive, impatient with detail.*

**You'll spot a D by:**
- Firm handshake; direct eye contact
- Talks first; interrupts; finishes your sentences
- Asks "what's the bottom line?" within minutes
- Office decor: awards, achievements, expensive but functional
- Calendar is full; meetings start late and end abruptly
- Email replies are 1–3 words

**What they buy:** results, time-saved, competitive advantage, status of winning, control

**What they fear:** being controlled, losing, wasting time, being seen as weak or indecisive

**How to sell to a D:**
- **Get to the point fast.** Headline, then proof. Skip the warm-up.
- **Bullets, not paragraphs.** They scan; don't make them read.
- **Bottom-line first.** ROI, time saved, win achieved — lead with the result.
- **Offer choices, not single options.** Ds want to feel in control — "Option A or Option B?" beats "This is what I recommend."
- **Pace fast.** If you're slower than them, they'll mentally check out.
- **Acknowledge their expertise.** Ds know they're smart; don't condescend.
- **Close directly.** "Ready to move forward?" — they respect the direct ask.

**What NOT to do with a D:**
- Don't make small talk for more than 30 seconds
- Don't over-explain or repeat yourself
- Don't show emotion early
- Don't be tentative or hedge
- Don't sell to relationships first — they care about competence first

### I — Influence / Influencer
*Outgoing, enthusiastic, social, optimistic, talkative, relationship-driven, can be disorganized.*

**You'll spot an I by:**
- Big energy, warm smile, comfortable physical contact
- Talks a lot; tells stories; goes off on tangents
- Asks about you personally before any business
- Office decor: photos with people, awards from peer recognition, casual
- Calendar is full of meetings *and* social events
- Email replies are long, expressive, exclamation marks

**What they buy:** experiences, recognition, fun, social connection, status visible to others, things they can share

**What they fear:** rejection, being ignored, boring outcomes, social embarrassment

**How to sell to an I:**
- **Match their energy.** Warm, expressive, engaged.
- **Tell stories.** Case studies as narratives, not as data.
- **Make it about people.** Who else uses this? Who's the team behind it? Who will they be connected to?
- **Compliment authentically.** Genuine recognition lands hard with Is.
- **Allow time for relationship.** Don't rush the personal connection.
- **Use testimonials with photos and names.** Social proof in human form.
- **Make the experience fun.** Lunch meeting > office meeting. Event invite > webinar.
- **Close around the experience and the recognition they'll get.** "You're going to love how this looks to your peers."

**What NOT to do with an I:**
- Don't lead with data, spreadsheets, or technical specs (you'll lose them in two minutes)
- Don't be cold or transactional
- Don't push them through the process too fast — they want to enjoy it
- Don't ignore their stories — they need to be heard
- Don't write long emails; they won't finish them

### S — Steadiness / Steady
*Calm, patient, loyal, harmonious, consistent, supportive, risk-averse, slow to decide.*

**You'll spot an S by:**
- Warm but reserved demeanor
- Speaks softly; listens more than talks
- Asks about you genuinely, including family
- Office decor: family photos, plants, comfortable not flashy
- Calendar has consistent patterns; reliable scheduling
- Email replies are thoughtful, polite, no exclamation marks

**What they buy:** stability, reliability, family security, harmonious products, things that don't disrupt their world

**What they fear:** sudden change, conflict, pressure, risk, letting people down

**How to sell to an S:**
- **Build the relationship first.** Don't rush. Multiple meetings are fine.
- **Speak calmly and steadily.** Don't bring high energy — it stresses them.
- **Emphasize stability and continuity.** "This is how it's worked for 15 years."
- **Use customer references that look like them.** Same demographic, same industry, same family situation.
- **Acknowledge risk and de-risk explicitly.** "Here's what could go wrong, and here's how we handle it." Doesn't scare them; reassures them.
- **Allow time to decide.** Pushing for same-day close usually backfires. Two or three meetings is normal.
- **Include the family/team in the decision.** Ss rarely decide alone on consequential purchases.
- **Close gently.** "Does this feel right?" works better than "Are you ready to sign?"

**What NOT to do with an S:**
- Don't pressure or rush
- Don't bring sudden surprises ("Actually, the price went up…" — they're gone)
- Don't be loud or aggressive
- Don't claim revolutionary change — they prefer evolutionary improvement
- Don't skip the relational opening

### C — Conscientiousness / Compliant
*Analytical, precise, detail-oriented, cautious, fact-driven, perfectionist, slow to decide, hard to convince.*

**You'll spot a C by:**
- Polite but reserved; minimal physical expressiveness
- Asks precise, specific questions — wants exact numbers, dates, specs
- Office decor: organized, clean, books, certifications, diplomas
- Calendar is meticulously kept; on time, sometimes early
- Email replies are precise, often with bullet points and references

**What they buy:** accuracy, quality, well-documented value, low risk, proven processes, things that meet exact specifications

**What they fear:** mistakes, being wrong, hidden flaws, surprises, sloppy execution

**How to sell to a C:**
- **Bring data, not stories.** Numbers, charts, methodology, sources.
- **Be precise.** Don't say "about " or "approximately" unless you mean it.
- **Document everything.** Written proposals, spec sheets, comparison tables.
- **Anticipate objections with prepared answers.** Cs respect preparation.
- **Allow time for analysis.** They will research. Encourage it; provide materials.
- **Reference detailed case studies with specifics.** "This client, this industry, this metric improved from X to Y over Z months."
- **Use technical language correctly.** Cs catch jargon misuse instantly and discount you for it.
- **Close logically.** "Based on the analysis, the path forward is X." Not emotional.

**What NOT to do with a C:**
- Don't hype, exaggerate, or over-promise
- Don't be vague — they translate vagueness as incompetence
- Don't use emotional or social-proof appeals as your primary lever (use them, but as support to data)
- Don't rush them; they need analysis time
- Don't ignore their detailed questions — they're testing you

## How to spot the type in 90 seconds

Watch for two axes in the first conversation:

**Axis 1: Pace** — fast or slow?
- Fast → D or I
- Slow → S or C

**Axis 2: Focus** — task or people?
- Task → D or C
- People → I or S

Combine:
- Fast + Task = **D** (Driver)
- Fast + People = **I** (Influencer)
- Slow + People = **S** (Steady)
- Slow + Task = **C** (Conscientious)

That's it. Within 90 seconds you should have a working hypothesis. Adjust as more data comes in.

## Common blended types

Most people are predominantly one type with a strong secondary. Common blends:

- **D/I** — the charismatic executive. Direct, fast, but warm. Common in sales leadership, founders, politicians.
- **D/C** — the strategic operator. Direct + analytical. Common in engineering executives, CFOs, surgeons.
- **I/S** — the warm collaborator. Outgoing but harmonious. Common in HR, account managers, teachers, hospitality.
- **S/C** — the careful loyalist. Stable + precise. Common in operations, accounting, government, family-business operators.
- **D/S** (rare) — the principled leader. Direct but loyal. Common in military, founder-operators.
- **I/C** (rare) — the analytical communicator. Outgoing but precise. Common in journalism, technical sales, expert witnesses.

Adjust the dominant style of your pitch for the primary; nuance it for the secondary.

## Puerto Rico calibration

DISC types exist universally, but **cultural defaults shift the visible distribution and the appropriate calibration:**

- **PR business culture rewards warm-relational behavior** even from task-focused types. A pure-D selling pattern that works in NYC will feel cold and aggressive in San Juan. Even when selling to a clear D, **invest 60 seconds in relational warmth** before going direct — it's the local equivalent of the handshake.
- **Many *dueños* present as S externally but operate as D internally.** Family-business owners often speak softly and warmly (S-coded) but make decisions quickly and decisively (D-actual). Read the decision pattern, not just the conversational style.
- **Cs in PR often have a relational layer mainland Cs lack.** A PR accountant will request the data *and* ask about your family. Provide both. Skipping the relational part loses the deal even if your data is perfect.
- **Is in PR are the social engine.** *El que conecta a todos.* If you can identify and serve the I in any organization or network, they will sell for you. Cultivate them.
- **Spanish-language register signals add a dimension.** Formal Spanish (*usted*, *don/doña*) plus DISC-S/C profile → highly conservative, slow-decision buyer. Casual Spanish (*tú*, first names) plus DISC-D/I → fast-moving relational buyer.
